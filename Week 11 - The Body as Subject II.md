# Week 11 – The Body as Subject II
## PHIL 402 – Introduction to Phenomenology
### Part III – Merleau‑Ponty and the Living Body

### Vocabulary Highlights
- **Lived spatiality**: Space as it is inhabited and oriented by embodied agency, not merely measured.
- **Objective space**: Space conceived as coordinates and positions among objects.
- **Body schema**: The integrated bodily unity as a potential-for-action that orients movement and awareness.
- **Static conception (of body schema)**: A summary mapping from experiences to physiological functions.
- **Dynamic conception (of body schema)**: The body as posture-toward-a-task in a situational space.
- **Figure–ground / point–horizon**: Gestalt structures organizing perception into salient figures and backgrounds.
- **Virtual movement**: A felt readiness or tension that discloses a body part as a power-for-action without actual movement.
- **Concrete movement**: Habitual, situation-tethered action in a familiar world.
- **Abstract movement**: Decontextualized, instruction- or concept-guided action.
- **Motor intentionality**: Pre-reflective, goal-directed directedness of the body toward the world.
- **Sedimentation**: Embodied, habitual know-how layered over time.
- **Spontaneity**: Live, adaptive responsiveness in the moment.
- **Dasein**: Heidegger’s term for the human being as the entity for whom being is an issue.
- **Gestalt**: Structured wholes in perception that are more than the sum of parts.

### 1. The Body’s Existential Space Compared to Objective Space
Merleau‑Ponty’s approach to embodied space is similar to Heidegger’s in that he thinks that the human being (**Dasein**) is not simply in space. We occupy, dwell, or live in space.
He differs from Heidegger in giving explicit voice to the fact that it is our body that inhabits this lived space:
> Let us begin by describing the spatiality of one’s own body. If my arm is resting on the table, I will never think to say that it is next to the ashtray in the same way that the ashtray is next to the telephone. The contour of my body is a border that ordinary spatial relations do not cross. This is because the body’s parts relate to each other in a peculiar way: they are not laid out side by side, but rather envelop each other (PhP, 100).

**Key Point:** Our parts are related in a peculiar way. They envelop each other in that they surround each other as a unified potentiality for action. Likewise, my entire body is not for me an assemblage of organs juxtaposed in space. I hold my body as an indivisible possession and I know the position of each of my limbs through a **body schema** that envelops them all (PhP, 100‑1).
**Body Schema:** The integrated unity of the body in virtue of which its various parts are understood by me as a potential for action.
### Two Different Ways of Thinking About the Notion of a Body Schema
- I. A **static conception**: The schema is a correlative unity that maps possible experiences in the body to physical functions (cf. PhP, 102). Merleau‑Ponty calls this a “summary” of how the body realizes kinds of experiences relative to expected relations to things.
- II. A **dynamic conception**: The **body schema** is dynamic. Precisely, my body appears as a posture toward a task, actual or possible. My body’s spatiality is situational rather than merely positional. If I stand at my desk and lean with both hands, only my hands are accentuated and my whole body trails behind them like a comet’s tail. I am not unaware of my shoulders or my waist; that awareness is enveloped in my awareness of my hands and of how they lean upon the desk (PhP, 102).

*Notes:* Merleau‑Ponty prefers II over I.
- Question: Why prefer (II) over (I)?
- Answer: The **static conception** fails to capture the richness of bodily experience and how we inhabit **lived spatiality** as embodied subjects.

**Argument**
1. In occupying space, my body inhabits a meaningful world in which my capacity for action is something I experience as a bodily subject.
2. The **static conception** treats the body only as an object correlated with physiological functions, a summary of mind–body correlations.
3. If a view treats the body only as an object, it cannot account for the ways the body shows up as a subject in experience.

C. Therefore, the **static conception** cannot account for the ways the body shows up as a subject in experience.
Consider “here” as applied to the body:
> When the word “here” is applied to my body, it does not designate a determinate position in relation to other positions or external coordinates. It designates the installation of the first coordinates, the anchoring of the active body in an object, and the situation of the body confronted with its tasks. **Bodily space** can be distinguished from external space and it can envelop its parts rather than laying them out side by side because it is the darkness of the theater required for the clarity of the performance, the foundation of sleep or the vague reserve of power against which the gesture and its goal stand out, and the zone of non‑being in front of which precise beings, figures, and points can appear (PhP 102‑3).

- So, the **dynamic conception** (II) is necessary because the **lived body** is a condition for the appearance of a world, not merely a series of experiences appearing to a disembodied subject.

**Another Argument**
4. All spatial access to the world is mediated by my **dynamic body schema**.
5. If (1), then my sense of **objective space** depends on my embodied existential orientation in **lived spatiality**.

C. My sense of **objective space** depends on my embodied existential orientation in **lived spatiality**.
*Objection (against 2):* **Objective space** is presupposed by the **Gestalt** structure of perception.
One might reply that **figure–ground** or **point–horizon** presuppose **objective space**, or that to experience a skillful gesture as figure on the body’s background, the hand must be united with the rest of the body by relations of **objective space**, making **figure–ground** a contingent content of a universal form of space (PhP, 103).
*Response:* What sense could “on” have for a subject who cannot be situated by the body in front of the world? It implies oriented space (up/down). Saying an object is on a table tacitly places me in the table or object and applies a category that fits body–world relations. Stripped of this anthropological contribution, “on” is not distinct from “under” or “next to.” Even if a universal form of space is a condition without which there would be no **bodily space** for us, it is not that through which there is **bodily space** (PhP, 103).
### 2. On the Status of Pathological Cases of Perception
We now consider pathological examples as evidence.
### The Patient “Schneider” (PhP, 105‑6)
Some symptoms:
- Incapable of performing **abstract movement** with eyes closed, i.e., movements not directed at an actual situation.
    - E.g., moving arms or legs on command, or extending and flexing a finger.
    - Cannot describe the position of body or head, nor passive limb movements.
    - When touched, cannot localize the point; does not distinguish two points even 80 mm apart; does not recognize size or form of objects pressed against the body.
- Executes **abstract movements** only if allowed to see the limb or perform preparatory, whole‑body movements.
    - Localization and recognition of tactile objects become possible with preparatory movements.

And yet:
- With eyes closed, executes habitual **concrete movements** swiftly and confidently.
    - Takes a handkerchief and blows nose, lights a lamp from a matchbox.
    - Makes wallets by trade at ~¾ normal output.
        - Can even execute these **concrete movements** on command without prep.

Summary:
- Dissociation between pointing and taking/grasping.
    - The subject who cannot point on command can quickly reach to the spot of a mosquito bite. Why the privilege of **concrete** and grasping movements? (PhP 105‑6)

**Key Distinction: Concrete vs. Abstract Movement**
- **Concrete movement**: utility‑based pragmatic capacities to interact with the lived spatial world with one’s body.
- **Abstract movement**: guidance of bodily action via cognitive or language‑based cues.

Claim: Schneider lacks **abstract movement** but retains **concrete movement**.
> The patient is conscious of **bodily space** as the envelope of habitual action, but not as an objective milieu. The body is available as insertion into familiar surroundings, but not as expression of a spontaneous and free spatial thought (PhP 106).

Question: What does Schneider lack? Is it just an abstract cognitive representation of the body as object?
Answer: No. He lacks something more.
> The normal subject immediately has several “holds” on the body… each bodily stimulation awakens not an actual movement but a sort of **virtual movement**; the addressed part escapes anonymity, appears through a strange tension, and as a certain **power for action**… The normal body can also turn away from the world and be situated in the **virtual** (PhP, 111).

Questions:
- What is “**virtual movement**”?
- What does the “strange tension” signify?
- What role does “**power**” play in differentiating abilities from Schneider’s?
- How does this extend Heidegger’s account of practical comportment?

### 2nd‑Pass: Positive Characterization of Abstract vs. Concrete Movement
Within the busy world of **concrete movement**, **abstract movement** hollows out a zone of reflection and subjectivity, superimposing a **virtual** or human space over physical space. **Concrete** is centripetal (within the actual). **Abstract** is centrifugal (within the possible or non‑being). **Concrete** adheres to a given background; **abstract** sets up its own (PhP, 114).
### On the Plastic Unity of the Subject
Merleau‑Ponty on relating pathological to normal cases:
> Illness, like childhood or the “primitive” state, is a complete form of existence… The normal cannot be deduced from the pathological… The genuine inductive method… treats phenomena as modalities and variations of the subject’s total being (PhP, 110).

What is rejected? Modular toggles of functions with others intact. Instead: a unified, integrated sensorimotor subject.
### 3. Motor Intentionality: Synthesis of the Embodied Subject and Its World
Against empiricism and intellectualism:
> Every physiological explanation generalizes into mechanistic physiology… intellectualist psychology levels out behavior… effacing the **abstract/concrete** distinction (PhP, 125).

> So long as the body is defined as in‑itself, it functions uniformly as a mechanism; so long as the soul is pure for‑itself, it only knows objects laid out before it… The **abstract/concrete** distinction belongs in the dimension of behavior (PhP, 125‑6).

- Empiricism atomizes experience and erases subjectivity.
- Intellectualism turns world into an accomplishment of consciousness.
- Both miss how embodied subjects sculpt a perceptual world via attention and motor agency.

### The Fluidity of Embodiment
> In my house I know, without discourse, that walking to the bathroom passes the bedroom, that looking out the window places the fireplace to my left… words between close friends carry references to shared dimensions without evoking past conversations (PhP, 131).

> The essence of consciousness is to provide itself with worlds… “world” as **sedimentation** + **spontaneity** explains Schneider across domains without reduction (PhP, 132).

Two key concepts:
- **Sedimentation:** embodied, habitual know‑how enabling fluid response.
- **Spontaneity:** live, skillful, in‑the‑moment responsiveness.

### The Unity of the Sensorimotor Subject
> **Motricity** as original intentionality. Consciousness is originarily not “I think,” but “I can.” (PhP, 139)

> Reaching for the telephone distributes tasks across hand, torso, and legs; combinations are given in advance as equivalent (PhP, 150).

- Vision, touch, and action are unified as skill; the unity differs across subjects and conditions, but persists as unity.
