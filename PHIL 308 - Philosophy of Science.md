## Course Description
*"There is a saying 'where science ends, philosophy begins.' If these two things are so disjoint, then how can there be a philosophy of science?"*
But there are questions concerning science, specifically about scientific knowledge, that scientists do not and probably cannot answer. For example, what makes one theory better than another, if we can never know with certainty if either one is true? How do we decide whether something exists if we'll never be able to observe it? In what sense is science objective when the scientists themselves are just as human and biased -- as the rest of us?
Further, what does it mean to 'do science'? How does this differ from other pursuits? Is there any role of the first-person perspective within science? What is a role of science in public policy making?
We will consider questions like these in this introductory course to which philosophy seems to be the only discipline that can offer some answers. Philosophy of science allows us to gain a more informed and critical view of science, scientific knowledge and scientific practice.
The main aim of this course is to introduce students to various classical and contemporary views and theories on issues in philosophy of science. A corollary is the aim to help students develop their own critical perspectives on these questions and associated critical reasoning and analytical skills. A further aim is to enrich our own critical understanding of these philosophical issues and their implications for our everyday lives.
Rather than studying these various theories and styles of thought as abstract bodies of knowledge, we will study them as embedded in the historical contexts in which they are developed. The aim here is to appreciate how philosophical positions and scientific theories are often shaped by various social and contextual situations of the philosophers and scientists.
*This course satisfies the DH and WI General Education Requirements.*
## Course Learning Objectives
At the successful completion of this course,
- Students will be able to define and explain key terms in the philosophy of science literature including hypothesis, observation, theory, experimentation, underdetermination, incommensurability, objectivity and diversity among others;
- Students will be able to explain with critical evaluation the main positions and arguments regarding central topics in philosophy of science;
- Students will be able to integrate and apply theoretical concepts from the course to understand everyday issues involving the role of science including public health crisis;
- Students will be able to formulate a critical and informed response to any questions about science's role in society and the role of values in science.

## Course Requirements
### Reading
The assigned readings for this course will be a mixture of classical and contemporary texts. Several of them might feel dense and relatively difficult. Often you will need to read the material more than once before the class meeting where we discuss that reading. Please allocate ample time to complete the reading. You must do all assigned readings before the class in which they will be discussed. *Read with curiosity, caring, and with an active and close attention.*
### Attendance, Class Participation and Engagement
To be successful this course requires your regular attendance, timely arrival, steady preparedness with the materials, consistent performance in in-class group assignments and writing assignments, participation in and reflection on in-class contemplative exercises, thoughtful contributions to class discussions, and willingness to work with others in fostering an open, respectful, positive and supportive learning environment where each member can fulfill their full potential to engage in philosophical pursuits.
I expect our discussion to be collaborative, curious and fearless. An ability to listen attentively and respond with civility to contributions from others will be paramount. This is not always easy. There might be times when an idea/hunch/position/argument proposed by an author we are reading or other member(s) of our learning community strikes you as deeply flawed and/or dangerous and thus without any merit. Academic integrity requires that you challenge such a position; that is the only way our intellectual community remains committed and honest to our collective pursuit. *But* civility requires two ground rules in this regard: first, please make sure that you have tried to understand that position; second, please make sure to target the position itself and not the person who expresses it (both typically save us from inadvertently committing ad hominem or straw man fallacies).
Preparedness for the class discussion includes having printed copy of the appropriate reading(s) with you at every class meeting. While I am interested in your *intellectual* presence and contribution, your intellectual presence presupposes your physical presence! You may miss up to 2 class meetings without penalty. But after that, **you lose 1/3 of a letter grade for your attendance, participation and engagement for every additional absence.** Students who miss class due to documented health issues, or to travel on university-sanctioned business, or for religious observances, are given excused absences for the class period missed. However, significant absences, excused or otherwise, may affect academic performance and grades in this course. So please get in touch with me right away if you have to miss a significant number of class meetings. Significant absences, repeated lateness or repeated early departure will affect academic performance and grades in this course. Further, because our schedule is subject to adjustment, make sure to consult the course site on Lamakū for assignments if you've been absent. **An absence from class never excuses you from work we did in class or work due for the following class.**
### Forum Posts
In Lamakū modules for 6 different weeks, there will be a designated weekly Forum Assignment. You are required to post a comment on that week's reading assignments and/or class discussion or a response to a prompt. Starting with Forum Post 2, your post could also be a response to a post from your classmate with the post you are responding to clearly indicated. You are expected to follow the Netiquette guidelines listed below quite closely.
**Netiquette**, or Internet etiquette, are guidelines for maintaining civilized, professional, and effective communication in the online environment and email exchanges. Facilitators and participants will demonstrate appropriate netiquette when interacting with each other. Written communication will be conducted using standard business English. Here are some basic guidelines:
- Keep your posts, questions and comments relevant to the discussion topic and within the scope of the course materials. If another participant posts a comment or question that is off topic, do not reply. I will reply in private to the participant.
- Take your posts seriously and review and edit your posts before sending.
- Treat the other participants in the forum in a polite and respectful manner.
- Model the same standards of behavior online you would follow in a face-to-face discussion.
- Don't repeat someone else's post without adding something of your own to it.
- Avoid short, generic replies such as, "I agree." You should include why you agree or add to the previous point.
- Always be respectful of others' opinions even when they differ from your own.
- When you disagree with someone, you should express your differing opinion in a respectful and professional way.
- Do not make personal or insulting remarks.
- Be open-minded.
- Do not use ALL CAPS when posting as this is considered "shouting."
- Do not "flame" others in forums. Flaming is the "act of responding in a highly critical, sarcastic, or ridiculing manner."

I reserve the right to remove posts that fail to adhere to these above guidelines judiciously. Such posts will also result in a lower grade zero for the Forum Posts. You are welcome to complete all (6) of the welcome Forum Post prompts. **I will count the best 5 to determine your final grade for the Forum Posts assignment.** Your Forum Posts will be graded on their:
**Quality and Relevance**: Excellent & Good: post thoroughly responds to the discussion prompt, reflects solid understanding of course content, and includes critical self-reflection; Fair: Post addresses the discussion prompt partially and reflects some understanding of the course content; Poor: Fails to address the prompt;
**Contribution to the Learning Community**: Excellent & Good: reflective, thoughtful and respectful post that offers creative and engaging approaches to topic; Fair: Minimum effort is reflected to engage with the learning community; Poor: No effort to engage with the learning community; and
**Basic Readability and Mechanics**: Excellent & Good: writing is clear, cogent and grammatically sound; Fair: Writing is unclear at times with noticeable grammatical errors; Poor: Writing is mostly unclear with numerous grammatical errors.
### Short Expository Essay
You are required to complete **2 Short Expository Essays (4-5 double-spaced pages with TNR 12 font and I inch margin each)** (or 1 Short Expository Essay and the SSCC project) in the course of the semester. The question(s) will be posted in the Lamakū Course module with the title "Short Expository Essay." There will be assignment links to upload your Short Expository Essay by 11:59 pm on the appropriate Sunday.
This assignment is designed to give you an opportunity to engage with a topic in depth within a few days of discussing it in class. In the short expository essay, I am looking for two things: First, a clear and cogent understanding of the main concepts and main arguments; Second, your sustained critical evaluation of that position and arguments offered in its support. An excellent essay should also reflect a familiarity with points and ideas discussed in class during the class period devoted to the related reading(s). You are welcome to write more than 2 essays (there will be 3 opportunities). In that case **I will count the best two to calculate your final grade. Every student must complete either Short Expository Essay 1 or Short Expository Essay 2** (or both). After that day *only one more essay (or the SSCC project) will be counted*. No late short expository essays are accepted and no short expository essays are accepted as e-mail attachments. Each short expository essay should have your name and the correct date. **I will evaluate the short expository essays as follows:**
Good: Demonstrates good engagement with the reading and the class discussion. Reflects some understanding of the main concepts and arguments.
Excellent: Demonstrates extraordinarily thoughtful, sustained engagement with the reading and the class discussion. Reflects clear and insightful understanding of the main concepts and arguments.
Poor: Too short to develop ideas, or way off the reading and/or class discussion; or technically sloppy as to interfere with my attending to what you have to say.
### (OPTIONAL) Science-Society-Citizenship Connection (SSCC) Project
This optional assignment gives an opportunity to critically think about how scientific knowledge and the knowledge making process involved intersect with society. One of the central challenges that the COVID-19 caused pandemic has brought to the forefront is how to balance individual freedom often associated with citizenship with scientific practices including public health concerns and recommendations. This assignment thus requires students to critically engage with one such location/occasion of intersection. Each student is required to:
a) find a newspaper article (published in a local, regional, national or international *credible* news outlet) that addresses this science-society-citizenship dynamics;
b) Write a report outlining 1) their reason for picking that article; 2) offering a brief summary of the article; 3) how their thinking has been impacted and transformed in the process of completing this assignment.
Please refer to the class period when this assignment will be distributed during week 10.
### Critical Essay & Critical Essay Presentation
You are required to write one critical essay (6-8 double-spaced typed pages with one inch margin) on an assigned topic. In your essay I expect you to pick one of the seven episodes from *The Golem*, and explore and examine critically how it reflects at least one (at most two) theme(s) from the theoretical part (i.e. the first 10 weeks) of the course materials. The quality of your essay will be judged by the following factors: a clear presentation of your thesis, a careful and coherent development of main arguments for your thesis, a reflection of the adequate understanding of your chosen episode from *The Golem* and your chosen theme(s), reasons for rejecting your opponents' arguments and finally a critical evaluation of the current status of the debate and/or topic. Please refer to the detailed guidelines to writing philosophy essays that will be distributed in class during Week 11.
A satisfactory completion of the critical essay presentation assignment will include a clearly organized presentation (5-7 minutes followed by a 2-3 minutes Q & A) to the class. These Presentations can be pre-recorded that you play during your assigned presentation time; you will need to respond to questions from class in person. Your Critical Essay Presentation is going to be during the Final Exam period.
### Self-Reflective Final Essay
This assignment is designed to give you an opportunity of self-reflection and self-examination especially in relation to this course. You are required to write a paper (4-6 double spaced pages) where you pull together different ideas, themes and arguments and practices encountered during the course of the semester and reflect on how they provided a location to engage with your own sets of beliefs and values on the nature of science. In particular, this paper requires you to explore how your understanding of science and scientific knowledge has evolved as a result of being in this course.
