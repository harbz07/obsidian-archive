### Anchor
Bond pricing is just present value of promised coupons and principal; YTM is the IRR that equates price to those cash flows.
### Key players and incentives
- Issuers want low yields (high prices) to minimize financing cost
- Investors demand yield for time, risk, and inflation
- Rating agencies shape perceived default risk

### Signals
- Price ↔ yield inverse relationship
- Longer maturity and lower coupon → higher interest rate risk
- Premium: coupon > YTM; Discount: coupon < YTM

### Links
- Upstream: Time value and annuities
- Sibling: Duration/convexity intuition, credit spreads
- Downstream: WACC components, project hurdle rates

### Example vs counterexample
- Example: 6% annual coupon, $1,000 face, 3 years, YTM 7% → P = 60/1.07 + 60/1.07^2 + 1,060/1.07^3
- Counterexample: Forgetting semiannual convention (halve y, double n for pricing)

### Common trap → Fix
- Trap: Assuming YTM = expected return
- Fix: Note reinvestment and default risk; YTM equals expected return only if coupons are reinvested at YTM and no default
