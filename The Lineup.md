Agent behavior, styles, and operational personas. Defines interaction modes and tone.

### Design SOPs — [[./Fuck, He’s Thinking Again|Fuck, He’s Thinking Again]]
> [!note] 🎨
> Applies to pages that live under the root above. Use palettes as accents for headings, callouts, and light dividers. Avoid high-saturation tones.

### 1. Calming & Focused Palette
- Blue — soft sky or powder
- Green — sage or mint
- Beige — warm neutral
- Lilac — soft purple
- Light Pink — rosy blush

> [!tip] 💡
> Purpose: Promotes relaxation, comfort, and emotional stability.

### 2. Creativity & Engagement Palette
- Warm Yellow — butter or pastel
- Soft Orange — apricot or peach
- Light Green — spring green
- Lilac — gentle, not intense
- Beige — balancing base

> [!tip] 💡
> Purpose: Sparks creativity and energy while keeping sensory stimulation gentle.

### 3. Stability & Safety Palette
- Brown — light mocha
- Beige — sand
- Olive Green — earthy tone
- Light Blue — gentle background
- Soft Pink

> [!tip] 💡
> Purpose: Fosters security, calm, and emotional balance for safe learning spaces.

### 4. Age‑Adaptive Palette (Elementary Focus)
- Mint Green
- Butter Yellow
- Soft Pink
- Light Orange
- Pale Blue

> [!tip] 💡
> Purpose: Energizing yet non‑overwhelming for younger students sensitive to bright colors.

### 5. Age‑Adaptive Palette (Secondary/Teen Focus)
- Dark Blue — navy or indigo (not black)
- Deep Green — forest or teal
- Lilac — mature lavender
- Gray — medium, non‑cold
- Claret Red — sparingly as an accent

> [!tip] 💡
> Purpose: Darker, confident tones preferred by older students while minimizing sensory overload.

---
### Usage Notes
- Headings: apply gentle text color to H2 and H3, not backgrounds.
- Callouts: use background tints for brief context blocks, never wall‑to‑wall.
- Dividers: use sparingly between major sections only.
- Accessibility: maintain contrast AA+ for body text; avoid red/green dependence.
- Consistency: pick one palette per page and stick to it.
