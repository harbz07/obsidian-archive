**Date:** October 2025
**Topic:** Problem of Other Minds, Epistemic Justice, María Lugones
## Abstract
This paper argues that evidentialist approaches to the problem of other minds (Argument from Analogy, Inference to the Best Explanation) fail as methods for knowing a cultural other's feelings because they exercise epistemic power and colonization. The demand for indubitable evidence is not neutral—it forces others to translate their reality into our terms.
The paper proposes María Lugones's "world-travelling" and "loving perception" as a relational alternative that replaces the demand for proof with the risk of proximity. This method demands vulnerability and sustained ethical commitment.
## Key Arguments
**Standard Approaches Fail**
- **Argument from Analogy (AA):** Generalizes from a single case (oneself) to all others; erases cultural and personal differences through projection
- **Inference to the Best Explanation (IBE):** Explains behavior, not feelings; treats the other as object to be explained rather than subject to be engaged
- **Clifford's Principle:** Demanding "sufficient evidence" for belief becomes violence when applied to people (e.g., assault survivors)

**William James's Corrective**
- Two imperatives: "Believe truth! Shun error!"
- Interpersonal trust is living, forced, and momentous
- Some truths are lived into or not at all
- Disbelief is not neutral but withdrawal of recognition

**Lugones's Method**
- **Arrogant eye:** Confirms the other only if they fit the perceiver's world; renders them invisible or interprets them as crazy
- **Loving eye:** Knows the other through generous, humble interaction
- **World-travelling:** "Seeing with the other's eyes… going into their world" to make identification possible
- **Playfulness:** "Openness to being a fool… not being self-important, not taking norms as sacred"

**Critical Limitations**
- Requires mutual participation and shared commitment to being known
- Demands vulnerability, time, and emotional labor
- Cannot guarantee success; reduces but doesn't eliminate risk of misunderstanding
- Requires permission and correction from the perceived

## Personal Narrative
The paper includes a first-person account of epistemic arrogance: a psychiatrist imposing a bipolar diagnosis over Harvey's self-understanding of ADHD, resulting in seven months of inappropriate medication, severe side effects, and profound harm. This exemplifies epistemic colonization—IBE that explains a person without asking them.
## Conclusion
The problem of other minds reveals the limits of epistemologies built solely on evidence and inference. The relational alternative defended here does not solve skepticism but reframes it: James grants permission to trust under uncertainty; Lugones teaches how to travel toward another without domination. To know another is not to prove them real but to acknowledge them as a participant in a shared world of meaning.
