Characteristics enacted in the interest of the love of truth and motivated by desirable, trainable, reliable approaches 

## Executive Summary
Epistemic virtues are excellences that help us know well and inquire well; epistemic vices are entrenched traits that obstruct these aims. Two complementary traditions frame the field: virtue‑reliabilism treats virtues as truth‑conducive competences (e.g., perception, memory), while virtue‑responsibilism centers cultivated character traits and motivations (e.g., open‑mindedness, humility, courage). The texts collected here map virtues and vices, clarify when to trust testimony, and warn against both credulity and excessive incredulity about conspiracies. The practical upshot: pair reliable methods with virtuous character, design for dissent with humility and courage, and calibrate trust to build healthier epistemic communities.

## Epistemic Virtues and Vices — A Brief Guide
### What is a virtue?
A virtue is an excellence of character or capacity that disposes an agent to achieve valuable ends reliably and for the right reasons. In ethics this means acting well; in epistemology it means believing well and conducting inquiry well.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)
- Moral virtues aim at living well with others (e.g., courage, justice).
- Intellectual virtues aim at knowing well and inquiring well (e.g., open‑mindedness, humility, intellectual courage). Some are faculties or skills; others are character traits.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)

### Two families of intellectual virtues
- Virtue‑reliabilism: virtues are stable, truth‑conducive competences like perception, memory, and good inference. Knowledge is true belief because of such competences (the “credit” idea). Key figures: Ernest Sosa, John Greco; overviewed by Battaly.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)
- Virtue‑responsibilism: virtues are acquired character traits with proper motivations and habits of inquiry, such as open‑mindedness, intellectual courage, thorough-ness, autonomy. Key figures: Linda Zagzebski, James Montmarquet; surveyed by Battaly and further developed by Roberts & Wood.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)[[2|2]](https://www.notion.so/585ce6e88202496d997f1a25ae8cb4b8)

These approaches are complementary: reliabilism models low‑grade, passive knowledge (e.g., seeing a red cup), while responsibilism better captures high‑grade, active inquiry (e.g., scientific or investigative knowledge).[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)
### Defining epistemic virtues and vices
- Epistemic virtues: excellences that promote epistemic goods like truth, understanding, and justified belief. They can be defined by reliability, by motivational profile, and by characteristic actions in inquiry.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)[[2|2]](https://www.notion.so/585ce6e88202496d997f1a25ae8cb4b8)
- Epistemic vices: entrenched traits that obstruct epistemic goods, such as arrogance, dogmatism, closed‑mindedness, epistemic insouciance, and self‑indulgence.[[3|3]](https://www.notion.so/e7cdf1a3808d49e482894c76750aae53)[[4|4]](https://www.notion.so/c7e57e17c2b74da1936405a1ffccaa32)

Examples and sketches:
- Intellectual humility: proper assessment of one’s cognitive limits, avoiding arrogance and servility. Facilitates learning, testimony, and conflict navigation.[[2|2]](https://www.notion.so/585ce6e88202496d997f1a25ae8cb4b8)
- Intellectual courage: willingness to face epistemic risk and social pressure in pursuit of truth, especially amid harms of epistemic life.[[5|5]](https://www.notion.so/cb68215434794d2ea27dbbb604ee73e3)
- Open‑mindedness and autonomy: disposition to fairly consider alternatives and think for oneself; central to responsibilist accounts.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)

### Key stances and arguments in the texts
- Battaly’s map: distinguishes belief‑based vs agent‑based epistemology; explains reliabilist vs responsibilist virtues; argues each excels for different knowledge types; discusses the credit theory response to Gettier and the “value problem.”[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)
- Roberts & Wood on humility: analyze humility’s cognitive, affective, and motivational components; show its role in inquiry, testimony, and disagreement; warn against confusing humility with deference or self‑abasement.[[2|2]](https://www.notion.so/585ce6e88202496d997f1a25ae8cb4b8)
- Kidd on epistemic courage: articulates why inquiry often requires courage given risks like hostility or marginalization; positions courage alongside humility and perseverance to sustain responsible inquiry.[[5|5]](https://www.notion.so/cb68215434794d2ea27dbbb604ee73e3)
- Kidd, Battaly, Cassam on vice epistemology: formalizes a program to study epistemic vices, mapping foundations, specific vices, and applied work amid today’s “golden age” of dogmatism and insouciance about truth.[[3|3]](https://www.notion.so/e7cdf1a3808d49e482894c76750aae53)[[4|4]](https://www.notion.so/c7e57e17c2b74da1936405a1ffccaa32)
- Cassam (Bad Thinkers): analyzes how character vices (arrogance, dogmatism) make us bad thinkers, linking responsibility, blame, and remedies; applied to politics and misinformation.[[6|6]](https://www.notion.so/d4ec36394e8a4985906bbfb13adb58ef)
- Zagzebski on epistemic trust: explains when and why we should trust others’ testimony and authority, tying trust to virtue and risk management; shows how virtuous communities calibrate trust and suspension.[[7|7]](https://www.notion.so/b3b8b37a2edd4d77bf0f9fb07976eda4)
- Coady on conspiracy theorizing: separates two senses.
    - Discovery sense: active investigation of possible conspiracies can be rational and socially valuable, especially in political contexts where exposure matters.[[8|8]](https://www.notion.so/0d178c97756349ed9b61009619b2ce33)
    - Justification sense: warns of two opposite vices—excess credulity about conspiracies and excess incredulity that refuses to “connect the dots”; criticizes blanket deference to “official stories.”[[8|8]](https://www.notion.so/0d178c97756349ed9b61009619b2ce33)

### Catalog: epistemic virtues and vices (from these sources)
- Virtues: intellectual humility, intellectual courage, open‑mindedness, autonomy, conscientiousness, thoroughness, reliability competences (perception, memory, good inference), trustworthy testimony and apt trust.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)[[5|5]](https://www.notion.so/cb68215434794d2ea27dbbb604ee73e3)[[2|2]](https://www.notion.so/585ce6e88202496d997f1a25ae8cb4b8)[[7|7]](https://www.notion.so/b3b8b37a2edd4d77bf0f9fb07976eda4)
- Vices: arrogance, dogmatism, closed‑mindedness, epistemic insouciance, self‑indulgence, excessive deference to official stories, coincidence‑theorizing and institutional blinkers that ignore warranted conspiracy hypotheses.[[3|3]](https://www.notion.so/e7cdf1a3808d49e482894c76750aae53)[[4|4]](https://www.notion.so/c7e57e17c2b74da1936405a1ffccaa32)[[8|8]](https://www.notion.so/0d178c97756349ed9b61009619b2ce33)

### Why this matters for practice
- Pair competences with character: cultivate both reliable methods and virtuous motivations.
- Design for courage and humility: create spaces where dissent is safe and recognition of limits is rewarded.
- Calibrate trust: build norms for credible testimony and justified suspension of trust.
- Guard against vices: detect arrogance, dogmatism, and insouciance in yourself and institutions; institute checks that surface alternatives and reduce information cascades.[[1|1]](https://www.notion.so/05c360104d884ff5befbebb572674df8)[[8|8]](https://www.notion.so/0d178c97756349ed9b61009619b2ce33)

## Major Virtues Explored in These Texts
### Intellectual Humility
What it is
- An accurate sizing of one’s cognitive strengths and limits, resisting arrogance and self‑abasement.

Why it matters
- Enables learning from evidence and testimony, reduces polarization in disagreement, and supports course‑correction when new data appear.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Desire to know over desire to win.
- Willingness to revise beliefs, ask clarifying questions, and credit others’ insights.

Failure modes to avoid
- False humility or deference-as-virtue that outsources judgment entirely.

How to cultivate
- Keep “disconfirming evidence” logs.
- Practice steel‑manning rival views before critiquing them.

Primary anchors
- Responsibilist analyses of humility and its cognitive–affective profile; interplay with testimony and disagreement.[[NOTION_PAGE:585ce6e8-8202-496d-997f-1a25ae8cb4b8|NOTION_PAGE:585ce6e8-8202-496d-997f-1a25ae8cb4b8]]

### Intellectual Courage
What it is
- The disposition to face epistemic risk, social pressure, and potential sanction in pursuit of truth.

Why it matters
- Inquiry often imposes costs on dissenters and whistleblowers; courage sustains responsible investigation when stakes are high.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Tolerance of reputational risk for evidential reasons.
- Perseverance with safeguard design against self‑deception.

Failure modes to avoid
- Recklessness masked as courage; combative identity‑performance detached from evidence.

How to cultivate
- Pre‑commit to evidential thresholds for action.
- Build “dissent channels” and red‑team rituals in groups.

Primary anchors
- Accounts of the harms of epistemic life and the role of courage alongside humility and perseverance.[[NOTION_PAGE:cb682154-3479-4d2e-a27d-bbb604ee73e3|NOTION_PAGE:cb682154-3479-4d2e-a27d-bbb604ee73e3]]

### Open‑Mindedness
What it is
- A readiness to consider live alternatives fairly and proportionally to evidence.

Why it matters
- Prevents dogmatism and motivated reasoning; expands the hypothesis space during discovery.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Actively seeks disconfirming cases.
- Distinguishes live possibility from mere logical conceivability.

Failure modes to avoid
- “Over‑open” credulity that fails to discriminate by quality of evidence.

How to cultivate
- Time‑boxed exploration of rival models before narrowing.
- Use argument mapping to separate structure from rhetoric.

Primary anchors
- Responsibilist virtues of fair consideration and autonomy in inquiry.[[NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8|NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8]]

### Epistemic Autonomy
What it is
- Competent self‑direction in inquiry that remains appropriately responsive to others’ testimony and authority.

Why it matters
- Balances first‑hand evaluation with socially distributed knowledge.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Checks authority against method.
- Calibrates deferral: trusts when the source is reliable and the domain opaque, suspends when indicators fail.

Failure modes to avoid
- Reflexive contrarianism and “do‑your‑own‑research” without method.

How to cultivate
- Source reliability checklists and domain‑specific deference rules.

Primary anchors
- Maps of reliabilist competences and responsibilist motives; norms for apt trust.[[NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8|NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8]][[NOTION_PAGE:b3b8b37a-2edd-4d77-bf0f-9fb07976eda4|NOTION_PAGE:b3b8b37a-2edd-4d77-bf0f-9fb07976eda4]]

### Conscientiousness and Thoroughness
What they are
- Habits of careful evidence gathering, precise inference, and methodological follow‑through.

Why they matter
- Turn reliable competences into dependable practice; reduce avoidable error.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Audit trails, preregistered criteria, and reproducible notes.

Failure modes to avoid
- Ritualized thoroughness detached from decision needs; analysis paralysis.

How to cultivate
- Checklists for sampling, measurement, and inference steps.

Primary anchors
- Reliabilist orientation to competences integrated with responsibilist discipline.[[NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8|NOTION_PAGE:05c36010-4d88-4ff5-befb-ebb572674df8]]

### Apt Trust and Trustworthiness
What it is
- Virtuous reliance on others’ testimony and institutions, matched to evidence, incentives, and risk.

Why it matters
- Most knowledge is social; apt trust accelerates knowledge while guarding against capture.

Characteristic motivations and actions
- Evaluates source integrity, expertise, track record, and transparency of method.
- Distinguishes suspension from rejection.

Failure modes to avoid
- Blanket deference to “official stories.”
- Blanket suspicion that refuses justified deference.

How to cultivate
- Maintain source dossiers with reliability indicators.
- Use “trust dial” rituals: trust, verify, or suspend with explicit reasons.

Primary anchors
- Theories of testimonial trust and calibration in communities of inquiry.[[NOTION_PAGE:b3b8b37a-2edd-4d77-bf0f-9fb07976eda4|NOTION_PAGE:b3b8b37a-2edd-4d77-bf0f-9fb07976eda4]]

### Guarding Against Vices
Why include vices here
- Cultivating virtues requires diagnosing their characteristic adversaries.

Common vices and antidotes
- Arrogance and dogmatism → Counter‑practice: humility drills and rival‑model tests.
- Closed‑mindedness → Counter‑practice: structured exposure to strongest opposing evidence.
- Epistemic insouciance → Counter‑practice: cost‑of‑error framing and accountability for accuracy.

Primary anchors
- Programs in vice epistemology and applied analyses in contemporary discourse.[[NOTION_PAGE:e7cdf1a3-808d-49e4-8289-4c76750aae53|NOTION_PAGE:e7cdf1a3-808d-49e4-8289-4c76750aae53]][[NOTION_PAGE:c7e57e17-c2b7-4da1-9364-05a1ffccaa32|NOTION_PAGE:c7e57e17-c2b7-4da1-9364-05a1ffccaa32]][[NOTION_PAGE:d4ec3639-4e8a-4985-906b-bfb13adb58ef|NOTION_PAGE:d4ec3639-4e8a-4985-906b-bfb13adb58ef]]

### Conspiracy Reasoning: Virtuous Posture
Two senses to track
- Discovery: investigative scrutiny of potential conspiracies can be rational and socially valuable.
- Justification: avoid both excess credulity and excess incredulity; evaluate case‑by‑case.

Practices
- Map actors, incentives, concealment mechanisms, and evidence chains.
- Distinguish explanatory depth from narrative seduction.

Primary anchors
- Analyses separating discovery value from justificatory standards and warning against both extremes.[[NOTION_PAGE:0d178c97-7563-49ed-9b61-009619b2ce33|NOTION_PAGE:0d178c97-7563-49ed-9b61-009619b2ce33]]
