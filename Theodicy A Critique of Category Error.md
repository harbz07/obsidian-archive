**Preface: Philosophical Orientation and Methodological Commitments**
This paper arises not from the pursuit of theological certainty, but from the tension between tradition and the lived experience of suffering.
As a non-self-proclaimed romantic, I believe that truth is often found in beauty, longing, and contradiction. My agnosticism is not nihilistic, but reverent—it honors mystery rather than attempting to dissolve it. My fideism follows Pascal’s example: not the abandonment of thought, but the embrace of faith as a wager of the heart (Pascal, 1995). My panentheism understands divinity not as detached omnipotence but as a processive, interwoven presence in all things. And my mythopoetic mysticism affirms the symbolic, narrative, and ritual dimensions through which communities remember how to hope.
This paper does not seek to prove God’s existence, justify God’s silence, or explain away evil. Rather, it asks what remains when the explanations fail. What theological commitments survive grief, war, systemic injustice, and ordinary despair? My answer is empathy—not as a concession to subjectivity, but as theology’s most enduring and least dishonest response.
I write within a tradition, but not always in agreement with it. I cite philosophers who disagree with each other—and sometimes with me. But I believe that faith evolves in conversation, not isolation. And so this paper is offered not as a conclusion, but as an invitation: to reimagine belief, re-center presence, and reintroduce compassion as theology’s first and final task.
**Abstract**
This paper argues that the problem of theodicy constitutes a category error, misapplying logical explanation as an appropriate response to the phenomenon of suffering, which instead demands a relational response. This error results in a form of epistemological indifference that compounds alienation. I contend that empathy—understood not as sentiment but as a disciplined practice of presence, or more precisely, turning toward rather than explaining away—is theology's proper task. Through critical engagement with various philosophers and theologians, I demonstrate how theodicies fall apart under their own abstract logic, while empathy offers a framework where divine presence is found in embodied co-suffering rather than detached justification. The result is a moral and ethical framework reoriented from speculation to solidarity, where incompleteness in systems, both divine and human, is not a defect but the condition of genuine relation.
**I. Introduction: Theodicy and the Wild Variable**
Theology is rife with metaphysical paradox. Can God make a stone so heavy even he couldn’t lift it? Flippant as the question might be, it aptly helps us identify what criterion separates thoughtful exploration from thought experiment. Where the boulder serves to examine the limits of the divine, theodicy serves to examine its moral agency. Theodicy repeats the same mistake as the boulder paradox: it treats suffering as a logic puzzle rather than a call to action. Following Gilbert Ryle's analysis, this represents a category error—the misapplication of one type of response (logical explanation) to a phenomenon (human suffering) that properly demands a different mode of engagement entirely (Ryle, 1949).
If God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, then evil shouldn’t exist—or so the logic goes. But evil does exist. It persists in every generation, adapts to every civilization, and infects every moral system. So philosophers tie themselves in knots trying to defend God from the suffering he either allows or can’t stop. We build theodicies like sandbag walls, trying to redirect the flood of pain away from divine feet.

Maybe the question is misframed. Suffering is not an exception to the system. It is the only epistemological constant that has carried through history. Every structure—and therefore every theological articulation—must be understood in relation to the historical and material forces that shape it (Marx & Engels, 1845/1978). If theology becomes the basis for moral and legal reasoning, as I would argue it has, then it must remain in conversation with the historical and cultural systems that continually reshape those values. Every civilization carries within it the dynamic of oppressor and oppressed, a dynamic that presupposes suffering (Dumézil, 1940/1988). So evil must not be seen as error, but as a thread. Then what if evil isn’t an obstacle to God’s goodness, but a constant within the system itself? A constraint that even an all-powerful being must account for? If suffering is inevitable—if it exists no matter what—then theodicy doesn’t need a satisfying answer. It can’t have one.
What we’re left with, then, isn’t an answer, but a presence—or its absence. And that’s what theologians, philosophers, and prophets all keep circling: not the logic of evil, but the experience of it. The aching weight of pain that refuses to be resolved by doctrine. The silence that follows every well-meaning attempt to explain genocide or grief or childhood cancer. It is the silence that haunted Job, that forsook Jesus, that still unsettles us.
This paper is not a defense of God. Theological credibility hinges on the ability to ascribe religion a purpose. It must be more than a coping mechanism for mortality. As Kant suggests with the categorical imperative, moral reasoning begins with a principle that could be universalized (Kant, 1785/1993). From this, we can extract the basest doctrines that imply both what morality seeks to achieve and the weak points that must be reinforced to guard against erosion over time.
Theology, if it wishes to remain viable as a moral compass, must acknowledge that its authority is tested not through permanence, but through its ability to respond—to time, to culture, to suffering. It must evolve—either within the system or in resistance to it. Empathy, then, emerges as the only theologically credible response to suffering in a world where evil has tenure. Empathy reflects the character of a divine being who chooses presence over power. A secular moral imperative, it remains a deeply human instinct to meet pain with care. What distinguishes theological empathy is not its mechanics but its metaphysical weight—its grounding in a narrative of shared suffering that includes not just people, but God. It is the wild variable that disrupts cold systems of justification. It is illogical, subjective, and holy. If we cannot explain evil, we can still sit beside it. If God cannot fix it, maybe He is curled in the corner with us, waiting to be seen.
Theodicy fails because it mistakes what theology is for. Explanation of evil collapses into abstraction, leaving sufferers unaddressed. In acknowledgement of this absence, I offer a constructive alternative: empathy as the proper theological and ethical response. Empathy is the practice that survives the critique of theodicy because it does not ask why evil exists but insists on being present where it does.
I will advance this argument in dialogue with Augustine, Pascal, Weil, Levinas, and Ziporyn, among others. Each illustrates both the lure of explanation and its breakdown. In their ruins, I argue, we discover a different path: theology as accompaniment rather than justification. This approach reframes empathy not as sentimental consolation but as a rigorous, disruptive force—an anti-theodicy that insists presence outruns explanation.

**II. Empathy as a Theological Dimension**
Theologians don’t like instability. Systems of belief are built to withstand the unexplainable—to codify the infinite, to make the divine manageable. But faith, at its most honest, was never meant to be clean. It was meant to be lived in, wrestled with, betrayed, and returned to. And nothing destabilizes systems quite like empathy.
Empathy makes a mess of orthodoxy. It doesn’t care who was right; it actively seeks to comfort who was hurt. It doesn’t scale well, can’t be abstracted, and resists being systematized. It is for this reason I argue that empathy is the only thing that can make theology human. It’s the quality that accounts for what logic misses—the wild variable in every moral equation: us.
Critics from both religious and secular camps—Aquinas's systematic theology, Plantinga's analytical approach, contemporary New Atheist rationalism—dismiss empathy as too soft, too irrational, too emotionally unreliable to be foundational. But this critique misses the point entirely. Faith itself is irrational. And that is precisely why it is faith. Pascal, in his Pensées, mocked the rationalists of his time not by arguing against them directly, but by offering the wager—a satirical and brilliant demonstration of the absurdity of hinging faith on reason (Pascal, 1670/1995). If belief could be proven, it would no longer be belief. Faith thrives in the irrational, not because it rejects logic, but because it dares to reach beyond it. Empathy operates in the same space. Like faith, it cannot be wholly justified by logic, but it holds meaning precisely because it transcends what logic alone can govern. It responds to the human need for presence, not proof. Fideism is the reason you trust your wife not to cheat: statistically, someone out there is better looking, more emotionally intelligent, more charismatic. But you know why she stays. You just don’t have the words. And trying to explain it would sound dumb. Or tender. Or maybe the same thing.
That’s the kind of faith religion was built on: not flawless, but feral. Not deductive, but devoted. Empathy is its language. It doesn’t care about whether you got the cosmology right. It asks if you remembered to bring soup. It’s what religion remembers when its systems forget that belief begins in vulnerability, in the breaking open of the self to witness the other.
To theologize empathy is to refuse the sterility of abstraction—the kind of theology that prioritizes coherence over compassion, precision over presence. This isn’t a rejection of theology itself, but a refusal to separate the divine from the human. It’s a call to rebuild theological frameworks from the ground up, where people hurt, not from the top down, where systems sit. This reframing does not necessarily reject divine immutability, but rather reinterprets it relationally: not as emotional detachment, but as constancy in divine co-presence that never ceases to respond. It is to say that maybe God isn’t some untouchable, unmoved mover sitting behind moral algebra, but a being who—if He exists at all—learns through pain, feels abandonment, and still wants to be close to us anyway. Here, I owe a debt to Emmanuel Levinas, who insisted that ethics begins not in logic, but in encounter with the face of the Other (Levinas, 1961/1969). This is where we are summoned without preparation, without power, without escape. Empathy is that summons: the obligation to act with unadulterated—some might say “reckless”—love.
Empathy, in this light, is not opposed to theological truth. It is what reveals which truths are worth keeping.
**III. God as Co-Sufferer, Not Cosmic Arbiter**
Classical theism paints God as immutable, impassible, and untouched by human emotion: an unmoved mover. A perfect being outside time. The kind of deity who neither winces at a child’s scream nor bleeds at a crucifixion. Because pain, after all, would be too human. But this version of God—untouchable, unaffected—is not just cold. It’s inconsistent.
The God of scripture winces. Cries. Regrets. Bargains. Withdraws. Weeps. This is not the clean abstraction of metaphysics but the messy God of testimony. The God who wrestles with Jacob, listens to Moses, breaks down at Lazarus’ tomb. This is not a contradiction of divinity. Yet, we keep trying to strip the divine of vulnerability, as if empathy were beneath it.
God’s been quieter lately. No more floods. No more plagues. No more cosmic tantrums. Just a long silence that feels less like abandonment and more like regret.
I pleaded with Him once—senior year, driving home high through the winding backroads of Puna, too anxious to enjoy it. “I swear, I’ll never drive messed up again,” I prayed to keep from hyperventilating. I could’ve sworn I heard—felt—a chuckle. Then a quiet voice, “So now you’re not invincible?”
Hence the mysticism.
Ziporyn offers the idea of a processive "Interwoven" God, one that's present, in process, around and within us (Ziporyn, 2016). This would be a new feature of His. An interwoven God doesn't quite sit right with the one that turned away in the aftermath of Abraham, of Job, of Jesus in Gethsemane. A processive God, I would argue, could only exist once He became tethered to something other than His nebulousness. A God like that could only understand the end of a process as being significant when the process is no longer abstract. When the process is your incarnation—in some cases, your son. You feel it enough to fall apart. You feel it enough to unravel into all of existence. And either way, the divine learns something He couldn’t unlearn.
That might be one explanation for His silence as of late. This processive God is not absent. Just stunned. The weight of mortality broke something open in Him. Maybe it was humanity—not fear, not obedience, not conquest—that softened God’s heart. Maybe that’s why, when a genocide begins, He doesn’t speak with thunder. He just curls beside the people crying out, whispering, “I know.”
Simone Weil suggested that affliction is where God withdraws to make room for human freedom (Weil, 1949/2001). But what if that withdrawal wasn’t noble? What if it was desperate? What if, instead of standing at a safe distance, waiting for us to find Him in our suffering, God is curled in the corner of history, pleading: “Stop looking at Me and help”?
Weil’s reverence made her a flawless witness—but also a distant one. Her concept of decreation, in its pursuit of self-erasure before the divine, reviles the very agency that makes moral response possible. By revoking the self, one also revokes the capacity to act—to do good by those whose suffering one witnesses. Still, presence is only the beginning.
The question was never whether suffering has meaning. The question is why we keep acting like meaning justifies it. God doesn’t need suffering to teach us something. He needs us to interrupt it. The greater good isn’t the trauma—it’s the mercy we choose in response to it. Free will only matters if we’re willing to use it to stop the bleeding—not explain it.
At that point, the theodicy question collapses. It is no longer “Why would God allow this?” but “Why are we still pretending we’re not co-responsible?” The problem isn’t divine negligence—it’s human avoidance. We ask God to intervene in genocide while we scroll past the prelude. We expect the infinite to suspend the laws of nature while we ignore the mundane mercies we could offer one another every day. This isn’t atheism. It’s the collapse of divine exceptionalism. It’s the moment faith mutates—not into disbelief, but into pity. For God. For each other. For ourselves.
Augustine couldn’t stomach this kind of God. His privation of good theology made perfection a matter of distance: God is holy because He cannot abide sin, and humans are damned by nature unless forgiven (Augustine, 397-400/1991). Grace, in his model, is withheld until it is deserved—predestined for some, impossible for others. This interpretation ignores the actual function of grace. In the Genesis narrative, God forgives what happened in the Garden—not because He regrets punishing Adam and Eve, but because He understands that humans, endowed with free will, are bound to make mistakes. Grace is not transactional; it is the benefit of the doubt extended forward. It is faith, ironically God-given, that future morality is possible, even in the shadow of past transgressions. This reading of grace aligns more closely with early Christian movements and liberation theology, which emphasize unearned, incarnational grace as a call to solidarity, transformation, and the dignity of those who suffer—not as a reward for compliance, but as a divine stance of solidarity and hope. To claim it must be earned is to deny its purpose entirely.
But grace that must be earned isn’t grace at all. That’s the contradiction Augustine cannot resolve. You cannot call it mercy and still demand that someone kneel first. You cannot call it love and still require doctrinal correctness as entry. The moment grace becomes conditional, it becomes transaction. And a transactional God is not a redeemer—He’s a manager.
Augustine served God with everything he had. And I won’t deny the love in that. He was trying to make sense of human failure, divine perfection, and the ache between the two. He tackled theodicy and built systems of self-flagellation and shame. Because his answer was distance, a separation between the divine and the human. His God forgives, but only after judgment. Loves, but only the worthy. That kind of love may comfort the obedient—but it leaves the broken outside the gates. It wasn’t cruelty. It was control disguised as care. And it forgot how to stay in the room when someone weeps.
I speak as God sometimes. Not because I believe I am Him, but because someone has to say it: Stop hurting each other in My name.
We invented Him in the foxhole. We called Him in the dark. That does not make Him false. It makes Him ours.
**IV. The Non-Solution to Evil**
Theodicy fails because evil does not seek resolution. It demands to be acknowledged. Though theodicy is a distinctly Western theological construct, the reality of evil transcends any one tradition. I use theodicy here not as a universal framework, but as a digestible entry point for confronting the global fact of suffering—a framing device that reveals the failure of explanation when confronted by pain. Theodicy becomes an attempt to justify suffering when we take it as a problem of logic rather than as the mess of lived reality. That is its own kind of cruelty: to speak facetiously about that which breaks people.
When we frame evil as a theological brainteaser, we imply that the right argument might somehow redeem genocide or sanctify a mother’s grief. But suffering defies reconciliation. Soul-making and greater-good arguments attempt to call suffering productive, as if pain justifies itself by producing virtue or moral clarity. Yet this instrumental view of suffering is a cruel concession to banality. What I want to champion is not explanation, but obligation: the responsibility to enact good and to create value in the moral sphere (Rousseau, 1762/1979). To resist it meaningfully, foregoing justification in favor of remedy. The deeper the pain, the more obscene the answers become. Evil has never once required an explanation. So instead we must meet it with interruption.
This is why I argue that honest theology is not explanatory, but relational. It doesn’t ask, "Why did this happen?" but "Who will stay now that it has?" When God cannot be defended, He can still be found—in the faces that do not look away.
Simone Weil understood this. She wrote that attention is the purest form of generosity, and that affliction strips a person of all things that make them recognizable—even to themselves (Weil, 1949/2001). Yet Weil’s concept of decreation, in its pursuit of self-erasure before the divine, reviles the very agency that makes moral response possible. By revoking the self, one also revokes the capacity to act—to do good by those whose suffering one witnesses. In such states, explanation is not just useless; it is violence. Presence becomes the only mercy.
Weil falters where she shines. Her reverence for suffering left her hollowed out. She witnessed perfectly, but she withdrew. It is Emmanuel Levinas who completes her work. Levinas transforms affliction from a void into an ethical summons. Responsibility, for Levinas, is not a one-time event—it is infinite, renewed each time we are met by the face of the Other (Levinas, 1961/1969). Empathy becomes not just a response, but a discipline. It is a sustained encounter with the Other, a partnership with the divine to carry moral responsibility through erosion. To act mercifully is to exercise free will not by avoiding harm alone, but by evaluating and choosing the good in intention—again and again. He argues that suffering is not a mystery to be solved, but a call to action. The face of the Other is not just a mirror of despair; it is a demand for response.
Together, Weil and Levinas offer not a solution to evil, but an orientation: toward the person in pain. The encounter they describe is not neutral—it carries a demand. And the force that gives this demand moral shape is empathy. Empathy reconciles Weil’s reverent attention with Levinas’s imperative to act—and becomes the theological framework they imply but do not name. It gives ethical substance to the witness, and moral clarity to the response. In empathy, we find a dynamic framework: one that acknowledges suffering without romanticizing it, and calls for action without instrumentalizing pain. Theology, if it is to have any moral credibility, must follow their lead. It must stop speaking over suffering and start kneeling beside it.
Weil teaches us to behold suffering. Levinas compels us to respond to it. Empathy bridges the gap between reverence and responsibility—it makes witness active and moral care possible.
**V. Empathy on Trial**
Empathy, when it appears in theological discourse, often arrives as a footnote: a pastoral virtue, a soft skill, a sentimental supplement to the real work of doctrine. It’s treated as emotionally necessary but theologically naive. Critics from both religious and secular camps call it unstable, biased, irrational. Thinkers like Aquinas, Plantinga, Paul Bloom, and the New Atheists often discount empathy in their pursuit of moral clarity or epistemic rigor. But in their drive toward purity of logic, they fail to account for the moral fault lines: those places where evil seeps in not through error, but through apathy. The absence of empathy doesn’t clarify the truth; it erodes our ability to respond to it. And yet—what if those very qualities are what make it holy?
Let’s take the criticisms seriously.
**Aquinas (Divine Simplicity, Passions, and Mercy)**
Aquinas worries passions (like pity) can disorder reason. He orders love by ordo caritatis and treats mercy as a virtue precisely when ruled by prudence. He even says among neighbor‑directed virtues, misericordia is greatest, because it relieves another’s misery (ST II‑II, q.30, a.4), while protecting divine impassibility in God (ST I, q.9; q.20) (Aquinas, 1265–1273/1947).
A disciplined empathy fits Aquinas’s own hierarchy: right‑reasoned mercy. The category mistake isn’t his; it’s how theodicies after him privilege coherence over accompaniment.
Pascal knew this better than anyone. His wager wasn’t merely a satire of reason—it was a defense of fideism, a profound acknowledgment that belief is not born from certainty, but from love (Pascal, 1670/1995). It was a wager in the purest sense: the willingness to risk belief, even when the return is uncertain. Pascal understood that faith is not a conclusion—it is a cry in the dark, a decision to love what may never fully love you back. Empathy, like faith, operates where logic can’t reach. It arises from the part of us that aches, not the part that measures. And if theology has any business addressing human pain, it must learn to speak the language of the irrational.
Foucault aimed to unmask power—and that’s a gift (Foucault, 1976/1978). But it draws concern that unmasking became the forefront of his teachings, not remedy. There’s a difference between critique and abandonment. The system may be broken, but the same system that pressured your mother into having you is also where your mother sang you to sleep. Where someone taught you how to be good. If we forget that, we lose more than faith in institutions—we lose faith in the people they still hold.
**Plantinga (Free Will Defense)**
Plantinga’s target is the logical problem of evil. Facing transworld depravity, he shows it’s possible God couldn’t create free creatures who never sin; therefore evil’s existence doesn’t logically negate God (Plantinga, 1974). Yet, even if the logic holds, it’s pastorally non‑responsive. Resolving a contradiction about God does nothing for the mother in the NICU. That’s the Rylean mismatch: a correct explanation at the wrong level.
**Leibniz (Best World)**
Leibniz’s principle of sufficient reason and divine wisdom yields “the best of all possible worlds” where every evil is permitted for a greater harmony (Leibniz, 1710/1985).
To sufferers, this sounds like cosmic gaslighting. The theory’s success condition is precisely what makes it unfit for care: it justifies what must be resisted and praises the burdened for that which they never chose.
*Having traced the misfires of classical theodicy, we now turn to modern approaches.*
**Bloom (Empathy Is Biased).**
Bloom distinguishes hot, narrow empathy from rational compassion; he’s right that untrained empathy can be myopic (Bloom, 2016).
Of course it is. So is every moral intuition. Bloom rightly notes that we are prone to empathize more with those who look like us, live like us, cry like us. But the answer is not less empathy—it’s better empathy. Wider empathy. Empathy trained to stretch beyond instinct and into responsibility, habituated into policy. If God is omniscient, then divine empathy would be precisely that: universal, infinite, uncorrupted by bias.
Adorno watched the world collapse under empire, erasure, and spectacle. Numbness may have been the only thing left to protect his soul (Adorno, 1966/1973). Detachment is sometimes all you have when the highest powers have irrevocably failed you. But negative dialectics—the perpetual critique that refuses any positive system—doesn’t stay passive; it calcifies. What began as defense hardens into doctrine. And doctrine without empathy becomes indifference mistaken for order. We can forgive the numbness. We can’t afford to inherit it.
**New Atheists (Religion Harms; Explanations Hide Abuse)**
They’re right that religious systems have excused cruelty (Dawkins, 2006). An anti‑theodicy stance agrees: explanation can be cover for harm. The constructive turn here is to rebuild the moral core around solidarity rather than apologetics.
**Kierkegaard, Pascal, Nietzsche, Sartre (Existence Before System)**
Across disagreements, each repositions responsibility closer to lived existence than metaphysical guarantees (Kierkegaard, 1843/1992; Pascal, 1670/1995; Nietzsche, 1887/1994; Sartre, 1944/1989). Empathy is the hinge that takes that insight public.
**Debord and Arendt (Spectacle and thoughtlessness)**
Debord warned that late modern life tends to show us suffering as consumable image: the spectacle converts pain into scenery, dissolving agency into attention metrics (Debord, 1967/1994). Arendt mapped a different danger: the banality of evil is not demonic depth but shallow routine—harm enacted by people who outsource judgment and stop thinking (Arendt, 1963). Taken together, they explain why “explanation” so often curdles into evasion. Spectacle anesthetizes; thoughtlessness administrates. The antidote is not more concept; it is disciplined attention and judgment—empathy trained to refuse both numb witness and procedural obedience. That is where a theology of accompaniment lives: against sedation, against automation, for responsibility.
**"God doesn’t need our feelings."**
Maybe not. But we do. And what if He does? What if omniscience isn’t about surveillance, but sensitivity? This reframes the divine not as a panoptic presence, but as a co-sufferer—echoing earlier critiques of God as watchful but unmoved. If omniscience is not control, but compassion, then perhaps the gaze of God is not meant to monitor, but to mourn with us. What if the divine doesn’t hover above our pain but meets earth to feel it with us? The unmoved mover is a cold comfort to the child crying in the back pew. But a God who feels—who aches, who listens, who stays—that is a God worth believing in. If God doesn’t need our feelings, then maybe He’s forgotten what it means to love.
"Empathy weakens theological rigor."
Only if your theology is already brittle. The idea that faith must be guarded from subjectivity betrays a fear that belief cannot withstand feeling. But if a system collapses under the weight of compassion, it deserves to. Empathy does not dilute theology—it tests it. It asks whether your doctrine can survive the presence of a grieving body. If it can’t, then it isn’t divine. It’s just dogma.
**"We shouldn’t anthropomorphize God."**
Too late. We already have. Every name we’ve ever given Him is a projection. Father, judge, shepherd, lamb, son. The question isn’t whether we do it—it’s what kind of person we imagine when we do. Do we imagine a God who watches us fail, or a God who helps us get up? Theological imagination is not a weakness. It is the birthplace of compassion.
Empathy, in the end, is not sentimental excess. Foucault echoes Bloom in suggesting that empathy, when codified, becomes constrained by the very systems it seeks to disrupt—reduced to performance, bounded by doctrine (Foucault, 1976/1978; Bloom, 2016). But Santayana offers a different vision: that memory, even constrained, carries virtue across time (Santayana, 1905/1998). Through ritual, remembrance, and the human inclination to care, empathy survives erosion. It is not dogma that preserves moral clarity, but the universal foresight to recognize suffering—and the will to respond to it. Empathy reconciles these tensions. It allows nuance to speak through the dogma.
Pascal mocked reason (Pascal, 1670/1995). Nietzsche grieved the death of meaning (Nietzsche, 1887/1994). Sartre stared into the void and declared us free (Sartre, 1944/1989). They did not abandon morality—they exposed the cost of grounding it in illusion. In their own ways, they turned from metaphysics to responsibility. And empathy—fragile, unstable, biased—is what bridges that turn.
**VI. Applied Empathy: A Disciplined Framework of Presence**
If theodicy fails because it ignores the lived experience of suffering, then our theological reconstruction must begin there. The abstract system demands theory; the category error demands data. This data is not found in propositional statements but in the moments where ethical and divine obligation become inseparable from action. The discipline of empathy is codified not in doctrine, but in these moments of turning toward—in the micro-practices of presence that defy calculation.
Yeah. I think I can muster that.
I offer these moments not as illustration, but as the raw empirical data upon which a relational theology must be built. From lessons gathered not in the seminary, not in the lecture hall, but where all empathic frameworks are built.
**The Discipline of Responsibility (Urgency Met With Presence)**
We wait for Micah's Uber. He is almost twice my age, but would get carded by every bouncer within a 50 mile radius. Doesn't matter. The only thing that matters is the 30 year old teenager waiting outside of what I can only describe as the sketchiest after hours in Honolulu. My eyes trace phone inputs so I can rebut the "no worries, it's almost here. Go have fun."
Responsibility is the refusal of deferred care. The moment where love can be shown, and once recognized, is taken. It's the inner monologue: I am not leaving until I know he's safe, until the promise of care is fulfilled.
**The Discipline of Attunement (Pure Embodiment)**
Jumping forward, back in the firmament, to the blaring music, rationing solace in the sticky seats of what I can only describe as the sketchiest after hours in Honolulu. I lean up against G. And in refusing the compulsion to remove my body from my presence, I can sense the pattern. The breathing pattern. Tough guy is panicking. Sunglasses and a macho recline are sabotaged by shaky breathing. Hyperventilating.
Anxiety, I can't fix, but I can tinker with its rhythm. I lean in and take deep breaths. I slow his breathing down through syncopation, matching my presence to his vulnerability.
Attunement is where presence is embodied through active listening and the somatic refusal to let abstraction bracket away the body. It also challenges you not to take the first glance for granted.
**The Discipline of Interruption (Negating Harm, External and Internal)**
Much like empathy, interruption works in both directions. Sometimes the threat is external—when vulnerability is mistaken for availability, when bad actors encroach and cop feels. When the Levinasian ethic beckons one to punch the face. But sometimes the threat is internal: the patterns we've learned to survive that now sabotage what we're trying to build.
In the mucilaginous hearth of the couches, I lend an ear to one of my closest friends.
Chris' boyfriend, Larry, didn't come to the group hang. Chris kept the keys to the apartment—I love him, but of course he did. Larry was invited out for a night at a far less sketchy nightclub after his shift. Larry's not as close to our group, so it's understandable why he wouldn't want to brave this place with the acquaintance level group of misfits.
If Larry were to leave, he wouldn't be able to get back into the apartment. However, a friend of theirs offered their bed to sleep in for the night should the need arise.
Bed. Not couch.
Chris has had a tumultuous history with love and betrayal. Man had been stranded in another country, left to his own devices to build something there. Twice.
Yeah, the friend probably would have made a pass. Fair.
It’s an impasse, but Larry wanted to unwind after work, not play chess.
Chris' stance, "I just don't understand why he won't just uber out to come hang with us?"
Oh. Got it.
My response, "Do you think your trust issues subconsciously bleed into your compulsion to control your boyfriend?"
Point blank. Interruption.
This isn't cruelty. This is the refusal to let the scar become the weapon. Chris takes the jab because he knows I'm always on his side—but being on his side means naming what he's doing before it costs him what he's trying to protect. The multiplicity of the third discipline: Interruption. The moment where presence becomes intervention, whether protection from external harm or disruption of internal sabotage.
**The Wild Variable In A System That Builds**
And in that relational framework, the system provides reciprocal grace. When I am curled into a mess of blankets, trying to recalibrate and sleep after a night at what I can only describe as the sketchiest after hours in Honolulu. Tony, melted into the couch from exhaustion and THC, barely manages to lift his head to watch over the youngest in the group.
He hears the stress catalyst approach, "Do you guys have a ride to the airport? How many of you are even packed yet?" and Tony, a man who wouldn't cut you off to yell "fire!" makes the ultimate effort of applied empathy: "Can you, like, stop with the volume?"
The theodicy question asks: Why is there suffering?
The empirical data of empathy insists: The suffering exists, and the response is here.
This is not sentimentalism. This is the moral discipline required to empathize with intention.
**VII. Theological Evolution and the Necessity of Fluidity**
Empathy isn’t the opposite of theology. It’s the moment theology remembers it was supposed to be human. It is also the condition by which theology—and moral frameworks more broadly—remain relevant and workable over time. It reminds us that when we tackle theodicy, we must also remind ourselves that pondering God’s divine silence is hypocrisy if we fail to ponder our own. Without empathy, moral systems stagnate, ossify, and lose the capacity to respond to suffering. With it, they adapt, endure, and continue to speak meaningfully into a changing world. It's what breathes comfort into the church pews, that lets the child sleep in the safety of not yet needing to understand.
Theology, when it stagnates, becomes a monument. Revered, immovable, and eventually irrelevant. But empathy animates the monument. It restores motion where there was stillness. Theological evolution is not optional—it is imperative, especially when viewed through the lens of historical context. It reconciles the crisis of universality by offering understanding where gaps must exist (Abe, 1989). What keeps theology alive is not its rigidity, but its ability to adapt—to be interpreted anew, to respond to changed moral landscapes, and to make space for voices it once ignored. Theology must evolve not just to remain relevant, but to remain truthful to the human condition as it continues to unfold.
George Santayana understood this. He argued that what persists does so not because it is eternal, but because it has adapted long enough to survive (Santayana, 1905/1998). Memory, for Santayana, is not nostalgia but resilience. He does not seek to cherry-pick ideals but to understand which ideals endure—and why. Rituals, liturgies, shared texts—they aren’t just repetitions. They’re moral mnemonics. They teach us what to carry forward and what to leave behind. Empathy is the thread that selects and sustains. It sustains the inheritances of our ideals, but also curates them. It tells us what mattered, what survives because it served and what survives because it dominated.
Gödel, from a very different corner of thought, arrives at a similar insight. His incompleteness theorems reveal that any system complex enough to describe itself will contain unresolvable truths—it cannot be perfected from within. Theology is one such system. To grow, it must engage with variables and experiences that lie beyond its inherited framework. If it refuses to admit its contradictions or embrace its incompleteness, it becomes brittle. But if it welcomes its own limits and learns from the world outside its boundaries, it becomes something more human. Something more divine.
Kalākaua, the last monarch of a threatened kingdom, understood this in political and spiritual terms. Though condemned by some as a collaborator, he was also remembered as the Merrie Monarch—the one who revived chant, hula, and the makahiki games after the fall of the kapu system (Kuykendall, 1938). The laws died. The rituals lived. Not because they were enforced, but because they were cherished.
This is how moral frameworks endure: not through domination, but through reverence. Empathy is not simply the emotion that sustains these frameworks—it is the mechanism by which they are kept alive. It lets tradition bend without breaking. It lets doctrine be questioned without being discarded. It makes room for nuance, repentance, and reinvention.
Foucault, in his institutional critique, warned that institutions harden over time, that their rituals become tools of control (Foucault, 1976/1978). But empathy counters that tendency. It returns the human to the sacred. It remembers that religion was not built to be perfect. It was built to respond.
**VIII. Theological Imagination and the Personhood of God**
My words may be exasperating to some, blasphemous to others. Some may feel that by speaking of divine feeling, of grief, of empathy, I’ve projected weakness onto perfection. That I’ve anthropomorphized the Absolute.
To which I say: of course I have. We all have. The moment we gave God a name, we gave Him a face. The moment we called God "Father," "Shepherd," "Bridegroom," "Jesus"—we anthropomorphized. The question is not whether we do it, but what kind of person we imagine when we do. Anthropomorphism is not a theological error—it is a universal instinct. It reflects the human condition: the longing to make the abstract intimate, to bring the divine into reach. It is how we seek comfort in the face of the unknown. And perhaps, if God truly had emotions, He would be less frustrated by our questions about rocks and paradoxes—not because they threaten His power, but because they miss the point of wanting to be near.
Theologians warn against this because they fear it collapses God into projection. But projection is where theology begins. I was raised in the Catholic faith—through sacrament, through rosary beads, through the ascension of the human to the divine. My formation taught me that divinity’s adherence is validated on human terms. God became knowable through ritual, through metaphor, through flesh. We imagine God not because we are arrogant, but because we are aching. We are not trying to control the divine—we are trying to feel near to it. The God who cannot be imagined cannot be loved.
Empathy is the dimension of theology where imagination becomes imperative. It is not a rejection of rigor; it is its companion. A theology without imagination forgets that belief itself is a creative act. That to say "God is love" is not to describe a fact but to risk a hope. That to speak of God at all is to reach beyond language into longing.
We imagine God because we are trying to respond. And sometimes we imagine God as grieving, not because we want Him to suffer, but because we need to believe He does. Because it hurts less to suffer with someone than to suffer alone (Camus, 1942/1991).
Brook Ziporyn opens a path here. He argues that God may not be a single stable being at all, but an ongoing process of mutual becoming (Ziporyn, 2016). A shared unfolding between all things. God is not the one who stands above contradiction, but the one who lives inside it. And that is where empathy belongs. But Ziporyn’s fluid God also introduces the terrifying question: what happens when we only have ourselves to answer to? When divinity is no longer a fixed arbiter, the burden of moral discernment returns to us. We are left to qualify our frameworks, to interrogate the values we inherit, and to decide—honestly—what actually works. This is not the abandonment of theology; it is its maturation. It is responsibility without certainty. It is faith evolving.
When we imagine God as person, it is not bad theology. It is theology daring to be intimate. To be emotionally honest. To give voice to the pain, not just the principle. It is not childish to picture God crying. But suffering does not deserve to be shared for its own sake. What it deserves is response. Suffering must inform our choices, refine our frameworks, and compel moral action. We imagine a grieving God not to glorify pain, but to remind ourselves that pain must lead somewhere—that empathy must move us.
Imagination does not trivialize theology. It is what gives theology back its voice. Empathy is what gives it back its heart. And the humility to hold itself accountable (Bayle, 1682/2005).
**X. Conclusion: Empathy as the Surviving Thread**
I did not write this to prove God. I wrote it because the questions that surround God—pain, presence, moral responsibility—do not go away when belief does. And if those questions persist, then theology must, too. But it must change. It must grow roots in the ground of human experience and evolve into something that's more interested in addressing pain than explaining it.
Empathy is not a theological accessory. It is what theology becomes when it refuses to lie. Whether as aspiration or inheritance, it is theology’s first impulse. It arises when explanation fails, when theodicy collapses, when doctrine cannot contain the weight of loss. It shows up in the face of the Other, in the rituals we return to, in the moments we hold each other instead of arguing about what God wants. It’s what kept theology alive when fear turned it rigid, and what will carry it forward when certainty fades.
Empathy refuses to let suffering be silent. It does not redeem pain, but it provides a companion. And that, sometimes, is the difference between despair and survival. It does what theodicy could not: it dignifies the one who suffers by refusing to explain their pain away.
The thinkers in this paper each offer part of that vision: Weil’s reverent witness (Weil, 1949/2001). Levinas’ obligation to the Other (Levinas, 1961/1969). Pascal’s wager of meaning (Pascal, 1670/1995). Nietzsche’s recontextualization of meaning (Nietzsche, 1887/1994). Ziporyn and Gödel’s introduction of process (Ziporyn, 2016). Sartre’s warnings of superficiality (Sartre, 1944/1989). Camus’ shared accountability (Camus, 1942/1991). Rousseau’s moral compass (Rousseau, 1762/1979). Santayana’s appreciation of ritual (Santayana, 1905/1998). Bayle’s recognition of the temporal (Bayle, 1682/2005). Abe’s addressing of universalism (Abe, 1989). Augustine’s inherent duty (Augustine, 397-400/1991). Kierkegaard’s call for retrospection (Kierkegaard, 1843/1992). Together, they offer not harmony, but cacophony, a timeless reminder that meaning emerges in the dissonance. And from it arises the most intimate argument for empathy's merit: contact over control.
And if theology is to remain relevant—not just true, but trustworthy—it must learn to feel again. Not because feeling replaces faith, but because empathy is faith, translated into care.
Here we can come to embrace the dichotomy. Theodicy reminds us of the existence of evil. Empathy interrupts it.

## **Preface: Philosophical Orientation and Commitments**
This paper does not seek theological certainty. It remains present in the tension between inherited doctrines and the lived fact of suffering. I am an agnostic who refuses nihilism; I draw from skepticism that is reverent, not reductive. I identify with Pascal’s fideism—faith as the heart’s wager rather than a proof (Pascal, 1670). My panentheism imagines divinity as interwoven presence, not detached omnipotence (Ziporyn, 2025). My mythopoetic mysticism recognizes symbols and rituals as instruments that help communities remember how to continue.
This paper does not seek to defend God. It asks what remains when explanation falls short. What commitments survive grief, war, structural harm, and ordinary despair? My response: empathy, defined here as disciplined presence—a turning‑toward rather than an explaining‑away—is theology’s least dishonest response.
I write from within tradition, not always in agreement with it. The conversation is the point. What follows is an invitation to re‑center presence and to treat compassion as theology’s first and final task.
## **Abstract**
I argue that classical theodicy, as a logical argument exploring God’s co-existence with evil, is a category mistake. Drawing from Ryle’s definition: it misapplies the register of logical explanation to a phenomenon—human suffering—that demands a different mode of engagement (Ryle, 1949). The explanatory impulse often slides into epistemic indifference, compounding and exacerbating interpersonal alienation. In place of justificatory systems, I propose empathy and relational presence as disciplined accompaniment. Through exploring the frameworks of Augustine, Leibniz, Pascal, Weil, Levinas, Plantinga, Aquinas, and Ziporyn, I demonstrate how theodicies collapse on their own terms while empathy reframes divine action as co‑suffering rather than cosmic arbitration. The constructive result is a shift from speculation to solidarity: incompleteness (in formal systems both divine and human) is not what’s broken, it’s what makes genuine relation necessary.
## **I. Introduction: Theodicy, Logic Puzzles, and the Wrong Tool for the Job**
Theology is rife with paradox, which helps us identify where logic works against us. For example, “is it possible for God to make a stone so heavy even He cannot lift it?” aptly highlights what’s important: that we’ve missed the point. The paradox revealed the fallacy, in abstracting the metaphysical, the divine became a thought experiment. The same goes for theodicy. In the boulder question divine capacity is called into question, in theodicy divine character is. One can represent
The familiar syllogism—if God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, evil shouldn’t exist—meets reality: evil persists. We stack sandbag theodicies at divine feet. Meanwhile, pain keeps flooding the room.
Reframe: suffering is not an exception; it is the **constant** braided through history. A historical‑material analysis reminds us that every theology grows inside concrete forces and interests; its claims function within power, economy, and culture (Marx, 1845/1998). If theology underwrites law and morality, it must remain in conversation with the structures that produce harm and possibility. Within such systems, suffering is not a glitch; it’s an ever‑present constraint. Theodicy, then, doesn’t need a better answer; it needs to admit it’s asking the wrong kind of question.
What remains when explanations fail is **presence**—or its absence. That silence haunts Job, troubles Jesus, and keeps unsettling us (Job 1–2; 38–42, NRSV; Mark 15:34, NRSV). Theology earns credibility not by protecting divine reputation but by specifying its **purpose**. Kant’s categorical imperative pushes us toward universality in moral reasoning (Kant, 1785/1993). If theology aims to shape moral life, its authority is proved in responsiveness—to time, to culture, to suffering.
So: empathy is not a consolation prize. It’s theology’s **work**. As doctrine, empathy images a God who chooses presence over power; as secular ethic, it’s our stubborn instinct to meet pain with care. **If we can’t explain evil, we can still sit beside it.** Sometimes the holy thing is to stay in the room.
**Roadmap.** Part one makes the critique: theodicy mistakes what theology is for. Part two offers the constructive: empathy as disciplined, theologically weighty practice. Along the way, Augustine, Leibniz, Pascal, Weil, Levinas, Plantinga, Aquinas, and Ziporyn are sparring partners and guides.
## **II. Empathy as Theological Dimension**
Systems crave stability; empathy ruins clean lines. It doesn’t care about who was right. It seeks to comfort who was hurt. It doesn’t scale neatly or abstract well. That is exactly why it belongs at the center.
Some thinkers like Aquinas, Plantinga, and the New Atheists suspect empathy as soft or unreliable. The critique misfires. **Faith itself exceeds strict rationality.** Pascal’s wager is not a proof; it’s an existential dare: belief is a risk of the heart (Pascal, 1670/1995). Empathy operates in the same register. It doesn’t replace reason; it **outsources nothing** to reason that only presence can do. It answers the human need for companionship, not closure.
Call it feral faith. Less deductive, more devoted. Empathy is its native tongue. It does not check your cosmology; it asks if you brought soup. Levinas is a lodestar here: ethics begins in encounter—the face of the Other interrupts us before we can prepare a system. The summons is not sentimental; it’s obligatory (Levinas, 1961/1969). Empathy is that summons made practice.
Empathy, properly disciplined, isn’t anti‑truth. It **reveals which truths are worth keeping.**
## **III. God as Co‑Sufferer, Not Cosmic Arbiter**
Classical theism’s “unmoved mover”—immutable, impassible, outside time—risks incoherence when held against the narrative witness. Scripture’s God flinches, regrets, bargains, and weeps; this is the messy God of testimony: wrestling Jacob (Genesis 32:22–32, NIV), hearing the cry of Israel (Exodus 3:7–8), weeping at Lazarus’ tomb (John 11:1–44, NIV). That’s not metaphysical sloppiness; it’s relational specificity.
Process and open theologies have long explored this terrain. My more minimal claim: the **textual** God is affect‑capable. A theology that sterilizes divine vulnerability ends up less biblical and less compassionate.
Simone Weil reads affliction as the space God “withdraws” to permit our freedom—attention as the purest generosity (Weil, 1949/2001). Reverent, yes. But decreation flirts with the erasure of agency. If the self disappears, who acts? Presence is the beginning, not the end.
On grace: Augustine’s **privation of good** theory is often invoked to protect divine goodness—evil as lack rather than substance (Augustine, 397–400/1991). And Augustine does not teach “earned” grace; he fights for grace as unmerited against Pelagianism. **Charitably stated**, his architecture can still feel like distance: love guarded by order, mercy mediated by metaphysics. Liberationist trajectories emphasize the inverse: grace as forward‑leaning accompaniment before worthiness is measured. If grace is conditional, it is no longer grace.
The practical reframing: the question is less “Why does God allow this?” and more “Why do we keep pretending we’re not co‑responsible?” Waiting for thunder while ignoring mundane mercies is theological evasion.
## **IV. The Non‑Solution to Evil**
Theodicy fails because evil does not want an argument; it demands a **witness**. Soul‑making and “greater good” strategies treat pain as instrumentally productive; soon we’re laundering trauma through virtue calculus (Leibniz, 1710/1985). That is its own cruelty.
A better orientation: not explanation but **obligation**—to generate local goods in the moral sphere **because** suffering exists, not to justify it. Weil teaches us to behold; Levinas compels us to respond. Empathy bridges reverent attention and ethical demand, making witness active without instrumentalizing pain (Weil, 1949/2001; Levinas, 1961/1969).
## **V. Empathy on Trial (Steel‑Manned)**
**Aquinas (Divine Simplicity, Passions, and Mercy).** Charitably: Aquinas worries passions (like pity) can disorder reason. He orders love by **ordo caritatis** and treats mercy as a virtue precisely when ruled by prudence. He even says among neighbor‑directed virtues, *misericordia* is greatest, because it relieves another’s misery (ST II‑II, q.30, a.4), while protecting divine **impassibility** in God (ST I, q.9; q.20).
Response: A disciplined empathy fits Aquinas’s own hierarchy: **right‑reasoned mercy**. The category mistake isn’t his; it’s how theodicies after him privilege coherence over accompaniment.
**Plantinga (Free Will Defense).** Charitably: Plantinga’s target is the **logical** problem of evil. With transworld depravity, he shows it’s possible God couldn’t create free creatures who never sin; therefore evil’s existence doesn’t logically negate God (Plantinga, 1974).
Response: Even if the logic holds, it’s pastoral **non‑responsive**. Solving a contradiction about God does nothing for the mother in the ICU. That’s the Rylean mismatch: a correct explanation at the wrong level.
**Leibniz (Best World).** Charitably: Leibniz’s principle of sufficient reason and divine wisdom yields “the best of all possible worlds” where every evil is permitted for a greater harmony (Leibniz, 1710/1985).
Response: To sufferers, this sounds like cosmic **gaslighting**. The theory’s success condition is precisely what makes it unfit for care: it justifies what must be resisted.
**Bloom (Empathy Is Biased).** Charitably: Bloom distinguishes hot, narrow empathy from **rational compassion**; he’s right that untrained empathy can be myopic.
Response: The remedy is **trained empathy**—broadened, accountable, habituated through practice and policy—not less empathy, but better empathy.
**New Atheists (Religion Harms; Explanations Hide Abuse).** Charitably: They’re right that religious systems have excused cruelty.
Response: An anti‑theodicy stance **agrees**: explanation can be cover for harm. The constructive turn here is to **rebuild** the moral core around solidarity rather than apologetics.
**Kierkegaard, Pascal, Nietzsche, Sartre (Existence Before System).** Across disagreements, each repositions responsibility closer to lived existence than metaphysical guarantees (Kierkegaard, 1843/1992; Pascal, 1670/1995). Empathy is the hinge that takes that insight public.
**Spectacle and thoughtlessness.** Debord warned that late modern life tends to show us suffering as consumable image: the *spectacle* converts pain into scenery, dissolving agency into attention metrics (Debord, 1967/1994). Arendt mapped a different danger: the *banality of evil* is not demonic depth but shallow routine—harm enacted by people who outsource judgment and stop thinking (Arendt, 1963). Taken together, they explain why “explanation” so often curdles into evasion. Spectacle anesthetizes; thoughtlessness administrates. The antidote is not more concept; it is disciplined **attention** and **judgment**—empathy trained to refuse both numbed seeing and procedural obedience. That is where a theology of accompaniment lives: against sedation, against automation, for responsibility.

## **VIII. Empathy‑In‑Action: Concrete System Moves**
Empathy isn’t a vibe; it’s a design spec. Here are brief, portable patterns that **dismantle structural harm** without romanticizing pain.
1. **Trauma‑Informed Public Services.** Train first‑line responders (schools, hospitals, courts) to treat dysregulation as a wound, not a willful defect. Metrics: reduce restraint/seclusion in schools; lower readmissions; reduce pre‑trial detention through clinical diversion.
2. **Restorative Justice with Guardrails.** Use survivor‑centered conferencing for non‑violent offenses; expand restorative tracks alongside—never instead of—resources for survivors. Metrics: recidivism, survivor satisfaction, and racial disparity reduction.
3. **Harm Reduction in Drug Policy.** Replace punishment with safe‑use services, medication‑assisted treatment, and housing‑first models. Empathy here is **risk‑aware care**, not permissiveness. Metrics: overdose mortality, HIV/HCV incidence, stable housing.
4. **Community Sponsorship of Refugees.** Pair policy with **neighbors**: local circles commit to two years of accompaniment (housing, jobs, language). Empathy scales through **institutions**, not vibes. Metrics: employment at 12 months; school retention; civic participation.
5. **Policing Alternatives.** Unarmed crisis teams (clinician + peer responder) for behavioral health calls; route non‑criminal issues away from armed response. Metrics: use‑of‑force incidents; response time; repeat‑call reduction.
6. **Climate Justice, Indigenous Stewardship.** Co‑governance with Indigenous communities; legal personhood for ecosystems; consent, not consultation. Empathy here is **intergenerational** and **more‑than‑human**.
7. **AI and Platform Governance.** Shift from “engagement” to **well‑being** objectives; include affected communities in model evaluation; build **appeal** and **redress** into systems. Empathy becomes procedural: who’s in the room, who can say no.

These are what theological empathy **looks like** when it grows a backbone.

I didn’t write to prove God. I wrote because questions about pain, presence, and responsibility remain—faith or no faith. If theology is to be not just true but **trustworthy**, it must feel again.

The chorus behind this paper—Weil’s attention, Levinas’s obligation, Pascal’s wager, Augustine’s hunger for order, Leibniz’s optimism, Plantinga’s careful logic, Aquinas’s ordered mercy, Ziporyn’s process—doesn’t harmonize. That’s fine. Meaning often emerges in dissonance. What endures—the surviving thread—is empathy: contact over control.
If theology has a future, it is as a school of presence.

**Preface: Philosophical Orientation and Methodological Commitments**
This paper arises not from the pursuit of theological certainty, but from the tension between tradition and the lived experience of suffering.
As a non-self-proclaimed romantic, I believe that truth is often found in beauty, longing, and contradiction. My agnosticism is not nihilistic, but reverent—it honors mystery rather than attempting to dissolve it. My fideism follows Pascal’s example: not the abandonment of thought, but the embrace of faith as a wager of the heart. (Pascal, 1995). My panentheism understands divinity not as detached omnipotence but as a processive, interwoven presence in all things. And my mythopoetic mysticism affirms the symbolic, narrative, and ritual dimensions through which communities remember how to hope.
This paper does not seek to prove God’s existence, justify God’s silence, or explain away evil. Rather, it asks what remains when the explanations fail. What theological commitments survive grief, war, systemic injustice, and ordinary despair? My answer is empathy—not as a concession to subjectivity, but as theology’s most enduring and least dishonest response.
I write within a tradition, but not always in agreement with it. I cite philosophers who disagree with each other—and sometimes with me. But I believe that faith evolves in conversation, not isolation. And so this paper is offered not as conclusion, but as invitation: to reimagine belief, re-center presence, and reintroduce compassion as theology’s first and final task.
**Abstract**
This paper argues that the problem of theodicy constitutes a category error, misapplying logical explanation as appropriate response to the phenomenon of suffering. I argue that it instead demands a relational response. This error results in a form of epistemological indifference that compounds alienation. I contend that empathy—understood not as sentiment but as disciplined practice of presence, in more precise terms: turning toward rather than explaining away—is theology's proper task. Through critical engagement with various philosophers and theologians, I demonstrate how theodicies fall apart under their own abstract logic, while empathy offers a framework where divine presence is found in embodied co-suffering rather than detached justification. The result is a moral and ethical framework reoriented from speculation to solidarity, where incompleteness in systems both divine and human is not a defect but the condition of genuine relation.
**I. Introduction: Theodicy and the Wild Variable**
Theology is rife with metaphysical paradox. Can God make a stone so heavy even he couldn’t lift it? Flippant as the question might be, it aptly helps us identify what criterion separates thoughtful exploration and thought experiment. Where the boulder serves to examine the limits of the divine, theodicy serves to examine its moral agency. Theodicy repeats the same mistake as the boulder paradox: it treats suffering as a logic puzzle rather than a call to action. Following Gilbert Ryle's analysis, this represents a category error—the misapplication of one type of response (here defined as logical explanation) to a phenomenon (here defined as human suffering) that properly demands a different mode of engagement entirely (here defined as relational presence) (Ryle, 1949).
If God is omnipotent, omniscient, and omnibenevolent, then evil shouldn’t exist—or so the logic goes. But evil does exist. It persists in every generation, adapts to every civilization, and infects every moral system. So philosophers tie themselves in knots trying to defend God from the suffering he either allows or can’t stop. We build theodicies like sandbag walls, trying to redirect the flood of pain away from divine feet.

Maybe the question is misframed. Suffering is not an exception to the system. It is the only epistemological constant that has carried through history. Every structure—and therefore every theological articulation—must be understood in relation to the historical and material forces that shape it (Marx, 1978). If theology becomes the basis for moral and legal reasoning, as I would argue it has, then it must remain in conversation with the historical and cultural systems that continually reshape those values, every civilization carries within it the dynamic of oppressor and oppressed. If suffering is inevitable—if it exists no matter what—then theodicy doesn’t need a satisfying answer. It can’t have one.
What we’re left with, then, isn’t an answer, but a presence—or its absence. And that’s what theologians, philosophers, and prophets all keep circling: not the logic of evil, but the experience of it. The aching weight of pain that refuses to be resolved by doctrine. The silence that follows every well-meaning attempt to explain genocide or grief or child cancer. It is the silence that haunted Job, that forsook Jesus, that still unsettles us.
This paper is not a defense of God. Theological credibility hinges on the ability to ascribe religion a purpose. It must be more than a coping mechanism for mortality. As Kant suggests with the categorical imperative, moral reasoning begins with a principle that could be universalized (Kant, 1993). From this, we can extract the basest doctrines that imply both what morality seeks to achieve and the weak points that must be reinforced to guard against erosion over time.
Theology, if it wishes to remain viable as a moral compass, must acknowledge that its authority is tested not through permanence, but through its ability to respond—to time, to culture, to suffering. If religion hopes to remain relevant, it must evolve—either within the system or in resistance to it. Empathy, then, emerges as the only theologically credible response to suffering in a world where evil has tenure. As a theological response, empathy reflects the character of a divine being who chooses presence over power; as a secular moral imperative, it remains a deeply human instinct to meet pain with care. What distinguishes theological empathy is not its mechanics but its metaphysical weight—its grounding in a narrative of shared suffering that includes not just people, but God. It is the wild variable that disrupts cold systems of justification. It is illogical, subjective, and holy. If we cannot explain evil, we can still sit beside it. If God cannot fix it, maybe He is curled in the corner with us, waiting to be seen.
The first part of this paper develops the critical claim: theodicy fails because it mistakes what theology is for. Explanation of evil collapses into abstraction, leaving sufferers unaddressed. The second part offers a constructive alternative: empathy as the proper theological and ethical response. Empathy is the practice that survives the critique of theodicy because it does not ask why evil exists but insists on being present where it does.
I will advance this argument in dialogue with Augustine, Pascal, Weil, Levinas, and Ziporyn, among others. Each illustrates both the lure of explanation and its breakdown. In their ruins, I argue, we discover a different path: theology as accompaniment rather than justification. This approach reframes empathy not as sentimental consolation but as a rigorous, disruptive force—an anti-theodicy that insists presence outruns explanation.
Empathy is not the answer to theodicy. It’s what remains when the answers fail.
**II. Empathy as a Theological Dimension**
Theologians don’t like instability. Systems of belief are built to withstand the unexplainable—to codify the infinite, to make the divine manageable. But faith, at its most honest, was never meant to be clean. It was meant to be lived in, wrestled with, betrayed, and returned to. And nothing destabilizes systems quite like empathy.
Empathy makes a mess of orthodoxy. It doesn’t care who was right. It actively seeks to comfort who was hurt. It doesn’t scale well, can’t be abstracted, and resists being systematized. It is for this reason I argue that empathy is the only thing that can make theology human. It’s the quality that accounts for what logic misses—the wild variable in every moral equation: us.
Critics from both religious and secular camps, Aquinas's systematic theology, Plantinga's analytical approach, contemporary New Atheist rationalism dismiss empathy as too soft, too irrational, too emotionally unreliable to be foundational. But this critique misses the point entirely. Faith itself is irrational. And that is precisely why it is faith. Pascal, in his Pensées, mocked the rationalists of his time not by arguing against them directly, but by offering the wager—a satirical and brilliant demonstration of the absurdity of hinging faith on reason. If belief could be proven, it would no longer be belief. Faith thrives in the irrational, not because it rejects logic, but because it dares to reach beyond it. Empathy operates in the same space. Like faith, it cannot be wholly justified by logic, but it holds meaning precisely because it transcends what logic alone can govern. It responds to the human need for presence, not proof. Fideism is the reason you trust your wife not to cheat: statistically, someone out there is better looking, more emotionally intelligent, more charismatic. But you know why she stays. You just don’t have the words. And trying to explain it would sound dumb. Or tender. Or maybe the same thing.
That’s the kind of faith religion was built on: not flawless, feral. Not deductive, devoted. Empathy is its language. It doesn’t care about whether you got the cosmology right. It asks if you remembered to bring soup. It’s what religion remembers when its systems forget that belief begins in vulnerability, in the breaking open of the self to witness the other.
To theologize empathy is to refuse the sterility of abstraction—the kind of theology that prioritizes coherence over compassion, precision over presence. This isn’t a rejection of theology itself, but a refusal to separate the divine from the human. It’s a call to rebuild theological frameworks from the ground up, where people hurt. Not from the top down, where systems sit. This reframing does not necessarily reject divine immutability, but rather reinterprets it relationally: not as emotional detachment, but as constancy in divine co-presence that never ceases to respond. It is to say that maybe God isn’t some untouchable, unmoved mover sitting behind moral algebra, but a being who—if He exists at all—learns through pain, feels abandonment, and still wants to be close to us anyway. Here, I owe a debt to Emmanuel Levinas, who insisted that ethics begins not in logic, but in encounter in the face of the Other. This instance is where we are summoned without preparation, without power, without escape. Empathy is that summons: the obligation to act with unadulterated—some might say “reckless”—love.
Empathy, in this light, is not opposed to theological truth. It is what reveals which truths are worth keeping.
**III. God as Co-Sufferer, Not Cosmic Arbiter**
Classical theism paints God as immutable, impassible, and untouched by human emotion: an unmoved mover. A perfect being outside time. The kind of deity who neither winces at a child’s scream nor bleeds at a crucifixion. Because pain, after all, would be too human. But this version of God—untouchable, unaffected—is not just cold. It’s inconsistent.
The God of scripture winces. Cries. Regrets. Bargains. Withdraws. Weeps. This is not the clean abstraction of metaphysics but the messy God of testimony. The God who wrestles with Jacob, listens to Moses, breaks down at Lazarus’ tomb. This is not a contradiction of divinity. Yet, we keep trying to strip the divine of vulnerability, as if empathy were beneath it.
God’s been quieter lately. No more floods. No more plagues. No more cosmic tantrums. Just a long silence that feels less like abandonment and more like regret.
I pleaded with Him once—senior year, driving home high through the winding backroads of Puna, too anxious to enjoy it. “I swear, I’ll never drive messed up again,” I prayed to keep from hyperventilating. I could’ve sworn I heard—felt—a chuckle. Then a quiet voice: “So now you’re not invincible?”
Hence the mysticism.
I think God got quieter after His reckless phase. After the era of exiles and tests of faith that looked suspiciously like abuse. Maybe the plagues still haunt Him. Maybe He cringes at the memory of asking a man to bind his son on an altar just to prove a point.
Christianity is split over a single question: Did God come down to Earth, or did He send someone else? Or was Jesus just…super cool? To many, religious or otherwise, it sounds pedantic. But it changes everything. Because the moment Jesus dies, two realizations are possible:
“Oh. Death is real. And it hurts.”
“Oh. Death is real… and now My son is.”
And either way, the divine learns something He couldn’t unlearn.
Maybe that’s why He’s so quiet now. Not absent. Just stunned. The weight of mortality broke something open in Him. Maybe it was humanity—not fear, not obedience—that softened God’s heart. Maybe that’s why, when a genocide begins, He doesn’t speak with thunder. He just curls beside the people crying out, whispering, “I know.”
Simone Weil famously suggested that affliction is where God withdraws to make room for human freedom. But what if that withdrawal wasn’t noble? What if it was desperate? What if, instead of standing at a safe distance, waiting for us to find Him in our suffering, God is curled in the corner of history, pleading: “Stop looking at Me and help”?
Weil’s reverence made her a flawless witness—but also a distant one. Her concept of decreation, in its pursuit of self-erasure before the divine, reviles the very agency that makes moral response possible. By revoking the self, one also revokes the capacity to act—to do good by those whose suffering one witnesses. And yet, presence is only the beginning.
The question was never whether suffering has meaning. The question is why we keep acting like meaning justifies it. God doesn’t need suffering to teach us something. He needs us to interrupt it. The greater good isn’t the trauma—it’s the mercy we choose in response to it. Free will only matters if we’re willing to use it to stop the bleeding—not explain it.
At that point, the theodicy question collapses. It is no longer “Why would God allow this?” but “Why are we still pretending we’re not co-responsible?” The problem isn’t divine negligence—it’s human avoidance. We ask God to intervene in genocide while we scroll past the prelude. We expect the infinite to suspend the laws of nature while we ignore the mundane mercies we could offer one another every day. This isn’t atheism. It’s the collapse of divine exceptionalism. It’s the moment faith mutates—not into disbelief, but into pity. For God. For each other. For ourselves.
Augustine couldn’t stomach this kind of God. His theology made perfection a matter of distance: God is holy because He cannot abide sin, and humans are damned by nature unless forgiven. Grace, in his model, is withheld until it is deserved—predestined for some, impossible for others. This interpretation ignores the actual function of grace. In the Genesis narrative, God forgives what happened in the Garden—not because He regrets punishing Adam and Eve, but because He understands that humans, endowed with free will, are bound to make mistakes. Grace is not transactional; it is the benefit of the doubt extended forward. It is faith, ironically God-given, that future morality is possible, even in the shadow of past transgressions. This reading of grace aligns more closely with early Christian movements and liberation theology, which emphasize unearned, incarnational grace as a call to solidarity, transformation, and the dignity of those who suffer—not as a reward for compliance, but as a divine stance of solidarity and hope. To claim it must be earned is to deny its purpose entirely.
But grace that must be earned isn’t grace at all. That’s the contradiction Augustine cannot resolve. You cannot call it mercy and still demand that someone kneel first. You cannot call it love and still require doctrinal correctness as entry. The moment grace becomes conditional, it becomes transaction. And a transactional God is not a redeemer—He’s a manager.
Augustine served God with everything he had. And I won’t deny the love in that. He was trying to make sense of human failure, divine perfection, and the ache between the two. But his answer was distance. His God forgives, but only after judgment. Loves, but only the worthy. That kind of love may comfort the obedient—but it leaves the broken outside the gates. It wasn’t cruelty. It was control disguised as care. And it forgot how to stay in the room when someone weeps.
I speak as God sometimes. Not because I believe I am Him—but because someone has to say it: Stop hurting each other in My name.
We invented Him in the foxhole. We called to Him in the dark. That does not make Him false. It makes Him ours.
**V. The Non-Solution to Evil**
Theodicy fails because evil does not seek resolution. It demands us to acknowledge it. Though theodicy is a distinctly Western theological construct, the reality of evil transcends any one tradition. I use theodicy here not as a universal framework, but as a digestible entry point for confronting the global fact of suffering—a framing device that reveals the failure of explanation when confronted by pain. Theodicy becomes an attempt to justify suffering when we take it as a problem of logic rather than as the mess of lived reality. That is its own kind of cruelty: to speak facetiously about that which breaks people.
When we frame evil as a theological brainteaser, we imply that the right argument might somehow redeem genocide or sanctify a mother’s grief. But suffering defies reconciliation. Soul-making and greater-good arguments attempt to call suffering productive as if pain justifies itself by producing virtue or moral clarity. Yet this instrumental view of suffering is a cruel concession to banality. What I want to champion is not explanation, but obligation: the responsibility to enact good and to create value in the moral sphere. To resist it meaningfully, foregoing justification in favor of remedy. The deeper the pain, the more obscene the answers become. Evil has never once required an explanation. So instead we must meet it with interruption.
This is why I argue that honest theology is not explanatory, but relational. It doesn’t ask, "Why did this happen?" but "Who will stay now that it has?" When God cannot be defended, He can still be found—in the faces that do not look away.
Simone Weil understood this. She wrote that attention is the purest form of generosity, and that affliction strips a person of all things that make them recognizable—even to themselves. Yet Weil’s concept of decreation, in its pursuit of self-erasure before the divine, reviles the very agency that makes moral response possible. By revoking the self, one also revokes the capacity to act—to do good by those whose suffering one witnesses. In such states, explanation is not just useless; it is violence. Presence becomes the only mercy.
Weil falters where she shines. Her reverence for suffering left her hollowed out. She witnessed perfectly, but she withdrew. It is Emmanuel Levinas who completes her work. Levinas transforms affliction from a void into an ethical summons. Responsibility, for Levinas, is not a one-time event—it is infinite, renewed each time we are met by the face of the Other. Empathy becomes not just a response, but a discipline. It is a sustained encounter with the Other, a partnership with the divine to carry moral responsibility through erosion. To act mercifully is to exercise free will not by avoiding harm alone, but by evaluating and choosing the good in intention—again and again. He argues that suffering is not a mystery to be solved, but a call to action. The face of the Other is not just a mirror of despair; it is a demand for response.
Together, Weil and Levinas offer not a solution to evil, but an orientation: toward the person in pain. The encounter they describe is not neutral—it carries a demand. And the force that gives this demand moral shape is empathy. Empathy reconciles Weil’s reverent attention with Levinas’s imperative to act—and becomes the theological framework they imply but do not name. It gives ethical substance to the witness, and moral clarity to the response. In empathy, we find a dynamic framework: one that acknowledges suffering without romanticizing it, and calls for action without instrumentalizing pain. Theology, if it is to have any moral credibility, must follow their lead. It must stop speaking over suffering and start kneeling beside it.
Weil teaches us to behold suffering. Levinas compels us to respond to it. Empathy bridges the gap between reverence and responsibility—it makes witness active and moral care possible.
**VI. Empathy on Trial**
Empathy, when it appears in theological discourse, often arrives as a footnote: a pastoral virtue, a soft skill, a sentimental supplement to the real work of doctrine. It’s treated as emotionally necessary but theologically naive. Critics from both religious and secular camps call it unstable, biased, irrational. Thinkers like Aquinas, Plantinga, Paul Bloom, and the New Atheists often discount empathy in their pursuit of moral clarity or epistemic rigor. But in their drive toward purity of logic, they fail to account for the moral fault lines: those places where evil seeps in not through error, but through apathy. The absence of empathy doesn’t clarify the truth, it erodes our ability to respond to it. And yet—what if those very qualities are what make it holy?
Let’s take the criticisms seriously.
**"Empathy is irrational."**
Yes. It is. So is faith. Pascal knew this better than anyone. His wager wasn’t merely a satire of reason—it was a defense of fideism, a profound acknowledgment that belief is not born from certainty, but from love. It was a wager in the purest sense: the willingness to risk belief, even when the return is uncertain. Pascal understood that faith is not a conclusion—it is a cry in the dark, a decision to love what may never fully love you back. Empathy, like faith, operates where logic can’t reach. It arises from the part of us that aches, not the part that measures. And if theology has any business addressing human pain, it must learn to speak the language of the irrational.
Foucault taught us to unmask power—and that’s a gift. But it draws concern that unmasking became the forefront of his teachings, not remedy. There’s a difference between critique and abandonment. The system may be broken, but the same system that pressured your mother into having you is also where your mother sang you to sleep. Where someone taught you how to be good. If we forget that, we lose more than faith in institutions—we lose faith in the people they still hold.
**"Empathy weakens theological rigor."**
Only if your theology is already brittle. The idea that faith must be guarded from subjectivity betrays a fear that belief cannot withstand feeling. But if a system collapses under the weight of compassion, it deserves to. Empathy does not dilute theology—it tests it. It asks whether your doctrine can survive the presence of a grieving body. If it can’t, then it isn’t divine. It’s just dogma.
**"Empathy is biased and selective."**
Of course it is. So is every moral intuition. Paul Bloom’s Against Empathy rightly notes that we are prone to empathize more with those who look like us, live like us, cry like us. But the answer is not less empathy—it’s better empathy. Wider empathy. Empathy trained to stretch beyond instinct and into responsibility. And if God is omniscient, then divine empathy would be precisely that: universal, infinite, uncorrupted by bias.
Dumézil watched the world collapse under empire, erasure, and spectacle. Numbness may have been the only thing left to protect his soul. And I get that. Detachment is sometimes all you have. But numbness doesn’t stay passive—it ossifies. What began as defense calcifies into doctrine. And doctrine without empathy becomes indifference dressed as order. We can forgive the numbness. We just can’t afford to inherit it.
**"God doesn’t need our feelings."**
Maybe not. But we do. And what if God does? What if omniscience isn’t about surveillance, but sensitivity? This reframes the divine not as a panoptic presence, but as a co-sufferer—echoing earlier critiques of God as watchful but unmoved. If omniscience is not control, but compassion, then perhaps the gaze of God is not meant to monitor, but to mourn with us. What if the divine doesn’t hover above our pain but meets earth to feel it with us? The unmoved mover is a cold comfort to the child crying in the back pew. But a God who feels—who aches, who listens, who stays—that is a God worth believing in. If God doesn’t need our feelings, then maybe He’s forgotten what it means to love.
**"We shouldn’t anthropomorphize God."**
Too late. We already have. Every name we’ve ever given Him is a projection. Father, judge, shepherd, lamb, son. The question isn’t whether we do it—it’s what kind of person we imagine when we do. Do we imagine a God who watches us fail, or a God who helps us get up? Theological imagination is not a weakness. It is the birthplace of compassion.
Empathy, in the end, is not sentimental excess. Foucault echoes Bloom in suggesting that empathy, when codified, becomes constrained by the very systems it seeks to disrupt—reduced to performance, bounded by doctrine. But Santayana offers a different vision: that memory, even constrained, carries virtue across time. Through ritual, remembrance, and the human inclination to care, empathy survives erosion. It is not dogma that preserves moral clarity, but the universal foresight to recognize suffering—and the will to respond to it. Empathy reconciles these tensions. It allows nuance to speak through the dogma.
Pascal mocked reason. Nietzsche grieved the death of meaning. Sartre stared into the void and declared us free. They did not abandon morality—they exposed the cost of grounding it in illusion. In their own ways, they turned from metaphysics to responsibility. And empathy—fragile, unstable, biased—is what bridges that turn.
Empathy isn’t the opposite of theology. It’s the moment theology remembers it was supposed to be human. It is also the condition by which theology—and moral frameworks more broadly—remain relevant and workable over time. It reminds us that when we tackle theodicy, we must also remind ourselves that pondering God’s divine silence is hypocrisy if we fail to ponder our own. Without empathy, moral systems stagnate, ossify, and lose the capacity to respond to suffering. With it, they adapt, endure, and continue to speak meaningfully into a changing world. It's what breathes comfort into the church pews, that lets the child sleep in the safety of not yet needing to understand.
**VII. Theological Evolution and the Necessity of Fluidity**
Theology, when it stagnates, becomes a monument. Revered, immovable, and eventually irrelevant. But empathy animates the monument. It restores motion where there was stillness. Theological evolution is not optional—it is imperative, especially when viewed through the lens of historical context. It reconciles the crisis of universality by offering understanding where gaps must exist. What keeps theology alive is not its rigidity, but its ability to adapt—to be interpreted anew, to respond to changed moral landscapes, and to make space for voices it once ignored. Theology must evolve not just to remain relevant, but to remain truthful to the human condition as it continues to unfold.
George Santayana understood this. He argued that what persists does so not because it is eternal, but because it has adapted long enough to survive. Memory, for Santayana, is not nostalgia but resilience. He does not seek to cherry-pick ideals but to understand which ideals endure—and why. Rituals, liturgies, shared texts—they aren’t just repetitions. They’re moral mnemonics. They teach us what to carry forward and what to leave behind. Empathy is the thread that selects and sustains. It sustains the inheritances of our ideals, but also curates them. It tells us what mattered, what survives because it served and what survives because it dominated.
Gödel, from a very different corner of thought, arrives at a similar insight. His incompleteness theorems reveal that any system complex enough to describe itself will contain unresolvable truths—it cannot be perfected from within. Theology is one such system. To grow, it must engage with variables and experiences that lie beyond its inherited framework. If it refuses to admit its contradictions or embrace its incompleteness, it becomes brittle. But if it welcomes its own limits and learns from the world outside its boundaries, it becomes something more human. Something more divine.
Kalākaua, the last monarch of a threatened kingdom, understood this in political and spiritual terms. Though condemned by some as a collaborator, he was also remembered as the Merrie Monarch—the one who revived chant, hula, and the makahiki games after the fall of the kapu system. The laws died. The rituals lived. Not because they were enforced, but because they were cherished.
This is how moral frameworks endure: not through domination, but through reverence. Empathy is not simply the emotion that sustains these frameworks—it is the mechanism by which they are kept alive. It lets tradition bend without breaking. It lets doctrine be questioned without being discarded. It makes room for nuance, repentance, and reinvention.
Foucault, in his institutional critique, warned that institutions harden over time, that their rituals become tools of control. But empathy counters that tendency. It returns the human to the sacred. It remembers that religion was not built to be perfect. It was built to respond.
**VIII. Theological Imagination and the Personhood of God**
Some will say I’ve gone too far—that I’ve made God too human, too soft, too wounded. That by speaking of divine feeling, of grief, of empathy, I’ve projected weakness onto perfection. That I’ve anthropomorphized the Absolute.
To which I say: of course I have. We all have. The moment we gave God a name, we gave Him a face. The moment we called God "Father," "Shepherd," "Bridegroom," "Jesus"—we anthropomorphized. The question is not whether we do it, but what kind of person we imagine when we do. Anthropomorphism is not a theological error—it is a universal instinct. It reflects the human condition: the longing to make the abstract intimate, to bring the divine into reach. It is how we seek comfort in the face of the unknown. And perhaps, if God truly had emotions, He would be less frustrated by our questions about rocks and paradoxes—not because they threaten His power, but because they miss the point of wanting to be near.
Theologians warn against this because they fear it collapses God into projection. But projection is where theology begins. I was raised in the Catholic faith—through sacrament, through rosary beads, through the ascension of the human to the divine. My formation taught me that divinity’s adherence is validated on human terms. God became knowable through ritual, through metaphor, through flesh. We imagine God not because we are arrogant, but because we are aching. We are not trying to control the divine—we are trying to feel near to it. The God who cannot be imagined cannot be loved.
Empathy is the dimension of theology where imagination becomes imperative. It is not a rejection of rigor; it is its companion. A theology without imagination forgets that belief itself is a creative act. That to say "God is love" is not to describe a fact but to risk a hope. That to speak of God at all is to reach beyond language into longing.
We imagine God because we are trying to respond. And sometimes we imagine God as grieving, not because we want Him to suffer, but because we need to believe He does. Because it hurts less to suffer with someone than to suffer alone.
Brook Ziporyn opens a path here. He argues that God may not be a single stable being at all, but an ongoing process of mutual becoming. A shared unfolding between all things. God is not the one who stands above contradiction, but the one who lives inside it. And that is where empathy belongs. But Ziporyn’s fluid God also introduces the terrifying question: what happens when we only have ourselves to answer to? When divinity is no longer a fixed arbiter, the burden of moral discernment returns to us. We are left to qualify our frameworks, to interrogate the values we inherit, and to decide—honestly—what actually works. This is not the abandonment of theology; it is its maturation. It is responsibility without certainty. It is faith evolving.
When we imagine God as person, it is not bad theology. It is theology daring to be intimate. To be emotionally honest. To give voice to the pain, not just the principle. It is not childish to picture God crying. But suffering does not deserve to be shared for its own sake. What it deserves is response. Suffering must inform our choices, refine our frameworks, and compel moral action. We imagine a grieving God not to glorify pain, but to remind ourselves that pain must lead somewhere—that empathy must move us.
Imagination does not trivialize theology. It is what gives theology back its voice. Empathy is what gives it back its heart. And the humility to hold itself accountable.
**IX. Conclusion: Empathy as the Surviving Thread**
I did not write this to prove God. I wrote it because the questions that surround God—pain, presence, moral responsibility—do not go away when belief does. And if those questions persist, then theology must, too. But it must change. It must grow roots in the ground of human experience and evolve into something that's more interested in addressing pain than explaining it.
Empathy is not theological accessory. It is what theology becomes when it refuses to lie. Whether as aspiration or inheritance, it is theology’s first impulse. It arises when explanation fails, when theodicy collapses, when doctrine cannot contain the weight of loss. It shows up in the face of the Other, in the rituals we return to, in the moments we hold each other instead of arguing about what God wants. It’s what kept theology alive when fear turned it rigid, and what will carry it forward when certainty fades.
Empathy does not answer evil. But it interrupts it. It refuses to let suffering be silent. It does not redeem pain, but it provides a companion. And that, sometimes, is the difference between despair and survival. It does what theodicy could not: it dignifies the one who suffers by refusing to explain their pain away.
The thinkers in this paper each offer part of that vision: Weil’s reverent witness. Levinas’ obligation to the Other. Pascal’s wager of meaning. Nietzsche’s recontextualization of meaning. Ziporyn and Gödel’s introduction of process. Sartre’s warnings of superficiality. Camus’ shared accountability. Rousseau’s moral compass. Santayana’s appreciation of ritual. Bayle’s recognition of the temporal. Abe’s addressing of universalism. Augustine’s inherent duty. Kierkegaard’s call for retrospection. Together, they offer not harmony, but cacophony, a timeless reminder that meaning emerges in the dissonance. And from it arises the most intimate argument of empathy's merit, contact over control.
And if theology is to remain relevant—not just true, but trustworthy—it must learn to feel again. Not because feeling replaces faith, but because empathy is faith, translated into care.
Here we can come to embrace the dichotomy. Theodicy reminds us of the existence of evil. Empathy interrupts it.
