## Course description
In this course, we will explore the philosophy of a school of thought that flourished in the early 20th century in Europe called “Phenomenology.” We will also look at some Existentialism as well as some of the criticisms that Phenomenology has endured from post‑Phenomenological thinkers.
We begin with a close look at Husserl’s founding of the phenomenological method across three periods: static, genetic, and generative phenomenology. We then explore Heidegger’s magnum opus, *Being and Time*, and its contributions to “existential phenomenology.” Next is Merleau‑Ponty’s *Phenomenology of Perception*, which recovers the centrality of the body. Finally, we consider several critical perspectives: feminist critiques of embodiment, interpersonal ethics as a critique of Heidegger, and hermeneutical and linguistic critiques of Husserl’s attempt to ground modern science.

### How to read
The readings are difficult. Read slowly and consistently. Plan your time, divide the pages across days, and aim for steady progress rather than last‑minute rushes. Re‑read where needed after attending lecture.
### Handouts
For each class, a detailed handout will be posted on the Lamakū course page the Sunday before the week’s meetings. Bring the relevant handout and readings to class. Handouts supplement but do not replace attendance.
### Paper submission guidelines
- Submit papers in person or as a .doc, .docx, or .pdf via email to the address above.
- Do not include a title page.
- Top‑right of page 1, single‑spaced:
Your Name
Student Number
Course Code
Due Date
Word Count
- Use a specific, descriptive paper title.
- Double‑space, 12‑pt font.
- Include page numbers and your name in the footer.

### Short Paper — 5–7 pp (30%)
**Topic:** Comparative analysis of Husserl and Heidegger, focusing on Heidegger’s critique of Husserl. Explain the critique in your own words. Assess its success and scope across Husserl’s different phases. Consider whether the Lifeworld addresses aspects of Heidegger’s critique.
**Instructions:** Do not attempt to answer every question above. Develop a clear thesis that takes a stand on their philosophical interaction. If you want a different topic, discuss with the professor first. You must email your thesis for approval before writing. Consult “How to Write a Philosophy Paper” on Lamakū. Questions can be handled via email or office hours.
### Final Paper Proposal — 2–3 pp (10%)
Provide three parts:
1. Opening paragraph with topic, clear thesis, planned argument, and brief roadmap.
2. Essay plan showing proposed structure (point form is fine).
3. Works cited with sources you plan to use.

Feedback will help you turn the proposal into a strong final paper.
### Final Paper — 10–12 pp (40%)
Choose any approved topic. You may expand your short paper. Obtain approval for thesis and essay plan before writing. Treat this as a major project.
> Important: On page 1, top‑right, indicate whether you want comments. If you do not explicitly request comments, you will not receive them. Request feedback only if you will use it to improve your writing.

### Weekly Writing Assignments — 10%
Submit five 1–2 page reflection papers via email over the term. These are graded for completion and are due Sunday evenings. Each reflection covers content scheduled for the following Monday and Wednesday. You cannot submit them all at the end; submit on a rolling basis before discussion.
### Class Participation — 10%
Attendance and participation matter. For every class, come prepared with a question or passage from the week’s readings. You may be asked to share to guide discussion.
> If participation is difficult for any reason, contact the instructor in office hours or via email to find an alternative. Accessibility services can also help; see Course Policies below.
