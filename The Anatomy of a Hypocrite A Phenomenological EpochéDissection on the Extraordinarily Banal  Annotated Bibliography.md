This annotated bibliography collects primary texts, assigned readings and critical secondary sources that illuminate the tension between Martin Heidegger’s existential call for authenticity and his personal embrace of Nazism. Each annotation briefly summarises the source, highlights key passages and explains how the evidence can help articulate your argument about hypocrisy, cowardice and the **banality of being**. Citations refer to the lines obtained from the referenced documents.
## Primary and Historical Documents
### **Heidegger, “Being and Time” (1927)** – *Primary philosophical text*
**Use for:** Defining authenticity, Dasein, anxiety and being‑towards‑death – the standards Heidegger fails to meet.
- **Authenticity vs. the ‘they‑self’.** The Stanford Encyclopedia explains that for Heidegger the authentic self is the **mine‑self**, while the **inauthentic self** (das Man) is the anonymous “they” that drifts along with societal expectations. Authenticity is not isolation; it is a modification of the **they** that involves confronting finitude and owning one’s existence[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2020/entries/heidegger/). Inauthentic Dasein reduces death to a generic event (people *“die”*), thereby fleeing the anxiety that reveals the unique possibility of one’s own death[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2020/entries/heidegger/). Such passages establish Heidegger’s claim that genuine existence requires stepping out of social conformity.
- **Being‑towards‑death.** In Division II Heidegger analyses death as the **possibility of the impossibility** of existence. Dasein cannot experience its own death but can relate to it as an omnipresent possibility. Anticipation of death constitutes the site where authenticity becomes possible[plato.stanford.edu](https://plato.stanford.edu/archives/fall2020/entries/heidegger/#:~:text=So%20far%2C%20Dasein%27s%20existence%20has,As%20Heidegger%20explains). These descriptions show that facing mortality demands courage and resoluteness – ideals Heidegger betrays in his political life.

### **Heidegger, “The Self‑Assertion of the German University” (Rectoral Address, 1933)** – *Public speech as rector of Freiburg University*
**Use for:** Evidence of Heidegger integrating Nazi ideology into his philosophy and repudiating academic freedom.
- In his rectoral address Heidegger rejects the liberal concept of **academic freedom** as a negative principle and insists that universities must bind themselves to the **destiny of the German Volk**. He ties students’ education to three “services” – **Labor Service, Military Service and Knowledge Service** – and organises students according to the **Führerprinzip**, subordinating scholarship to obedience and the state[la.utexas.edu](https://la.utexas.edu/users/hcleaver/330T/350kPEEHeideggerSelf-Assertion.pdf#:~:text=their%20essence%20and%20thereby%20first,will%20unfold%20from%20this%20truth). He frames the task of the university as an *assertion of its spiritual mission* aligned with National Socialism[la.utexas.edu](https://la.utexas.edu/users/hcleaver/330T/350kPEEHeideggerSelf-Assertion.pdf#:~:text=that%20arise%20from%20it%20%E2%80%93,the%20state%E2%80%99s%20destiny%20that%20always). This document demonstrates that Heidegger’s call for authenticity mutated into advocacy for authoritarian conformity.

### **Heidegger, *****Black Notebooks***** (1931–1941)** – *Private notebooks revealing anti‑Semitism and political thinking*
**Use for:** Showing that Heidegger’s antisemitism is not incidental but integrated into his philosophical outlook.
- The notebooks include reflections where Heidegger portrays **“world Judaism”** as a driver of Western modernity and links Jews’ perceived “talent for calculation” to the decline of spiritual life. A Guardian report summarising the notebooks notes that he complained Jews did not need to fight in wars to influence society and processed antisemitic ideas philosophically[theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2014/mar/13/martin-heidegger-black-notebooks-reveal-nazi-ideology-antisemitism#:~:text=This%20week%27s%20publication%20of%20the,to%20stop%20the%20notebooks%27%20publication)[theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2014/mar/13/martin-heidegger-black-notebooks-reveal-nazi-ideology-antisemitism#:~:text=theories%20because%20,the%20race%20principle%20for%20longest). Editor Peter Trawny observed that Heidegger integrated antisemitism into his thought and associated it with a resentment toward American and English culture[theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2014/mar/13/martin-heidegger-black-notebooks-reveal-nazi-ideology-antisemitism#:~:text=,Peter%20Trawny%2C%20told%20the%20Guardian). These passages reveal a worldview steeped in racist generalisation that contradicts his professed sensitivity to being‑in‑the‑world.

### **Heidegger, “Only a God Can Save Us” (Der Spiegel interview, 1966; published 1976)** – *Postwar self‑justification*
**Use for:** Demonstrating Heidegger’s evasions and rationalisations after the war.
- In a 1966 interview with *Der Spiegel*, Heidegger portrays himself as an apolitical scholar forced into an administrative role. He claims he refused to post a “Jewish notice” and threatened to resign if required to replace deans with Nazi Party members[la.utexas.edu](https://la.utexas.edu/users/hcleaver/330T/350kPEEHeideggerSpiegel.pdf#:~:text=already%20clear%20to%20me%20that,only%20ten%20months%20in%20office); he describes having his lectures suppressed and being under constant surveillance[la.utexas.edu](https://la.utexas.edu/users/hcleaver/330T/350kPEEHeideggerSpiegel.pdf#:~:text=SPIEGEL%3A%20How%20did%20the%20transfer,I%20was%20constantly%20under%20surveillance). These statements show how, after the war, Heidegger recast his silence as resistance, refusing to confront the moral implications of his collaboration.

## Secondary Critical Literature
### **Nancy, Jean‑Luc. “The Banality of Heidegger” (2015)** – *Monograph analysing the Black Notebooks*
**Use for:** Framing Heidegger’s antisemitism as philosophically significant and linking it to Arendt’s concept of banality.
- Nancy argues that the Black Notebooks show Heidegger’s antisemitism is not limited to isolated remarks but constitutes a structural element of his thought. He rejects the idea that a “small amount” of antisemitism can be dismissed; any such prejudice, however minor, occupies a **world‑historical plane**[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/the-banality-of-heidegger-9780823275953.html). Nancy contends that our task is neither to defend nor to dismiss Heidegger but to analyse how antisemitism operates within his thinking and reflects a broader **Western self‑hatred**[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/the-banality-of-heidegger-9780823275953.html#:~:text=a%20defensive%20argument%20from%20Nancy,a%20movement%2C%20I%20would%20xii)[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/the-banality-of-heidegger-9780823275953.html#:~:text=this%29,to%20show%20in%20its%20most). This critical stance supports your claim that Heidegger’s hypocrisy is not a biographical aberration but a philosophical malignancy.

### **Pushpakumara, Saman. “Martin Heidegger and Philosophical Nazism” (2022)** – *Academic article on ResearchGate*
**Use for:** Presenting the argument that Heidegger’s Nazism is inherent to his ontology.
- Pushpakumara asserts that publication of the Black Notebooks confirms Heidegger’s deep involvement with Nazism and antisemitism. He argues that Heidegger’s **Nazi engagement is inherent to his philosophical ontology and political subjectivity**, not a historical accident[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/362777445_Martin_Heidegger_and_philosophical_Nazism#:~:text=Dr). Earlier critics such as Husserl, György Lukács, Karl Löwith and Walter Benjamin identified fascist elements in his work, whereas defenders like Derrida sought to separate the philosophy from the politics[researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/362777445_Martin_Heidegger_and_philosophical_Nazism#:~:text=connection%20is%20sustained%20end%20of,Semite). This source underscores how the contradiction between Heidegger’s teachings and actions originates from within his ontological project.

### **Trawny, Peter. *****Heidegger and the Myth of the Jewish World Conspiracy***** (2015)** – *Editor’s commentary on the Black Notebooks (not available in full)*
**Use for:** Background on how Heidegger philosophically reworked antisemitic stereotypes.
- Trawny’s commentary (summarised in the Guardian article) highlights that Heidegger absorbed antisemitic stereotypes and merged them with his critique of modernity. He emphasises that Heidegger’s antisemitism is not merely social prejudice but is presented as a philosophical insight into Western decline[theguardian.com](https://www.theguardian.com/books/2014/mar/13/martin-heidegger-black-notebooks-reveal-nazi-ideology-antisemitism#:~:text=,Peter%20Trawny%2C%20told%20the%20Guardian). Although the full text may be difficult to access, referencing it shows that scholars directly involved in editing the notebooks view antisemitism as philosophically embedded.

### **Arendt, Hannah. “Labor, Work, Action” (1958)** – *Essay distinguishing forms of human activity*
**Use for:** Contrasting Heidegger’s contemplative focus with Arendt’s elevation of action and her notion of banality.
- Arendt distinguishes **labor** (maintenance of life), **work** (making durable things) and **action** (public political engagement). She notes that while classical philosophy placed the highest value on contemplation, active life is essential because humans “cannot live only by contemplating,” and the vita activa supplies the conditions for living[hiw.kuleuven.be](https://hiw.kuleuven.be/en/apply/2324-academic-reading-and-writing-test-text.pdf#:~:text=LABOR%2C%20WORK%2C%20ACTION%20Hannah%20Arendt,contest%20the%20traditional%20assumption%20that). In modern times, she argues, society perversely glorifies labor while neglecting the higher activities of work and action[hiw.kuleuven.be](https://hiw.kuleuven.be/en/apply/2324-academic-reading-and-writing-test-text.pdf#:~:text=turbs%20the%20necessary%20quiet%20so,29). When read alongside Heidegger’s emphasis on authenticity and contemplation, Arendt’s essay provides a critical perspective: an authentic life must include courageous action in the world, not just introspection. Her later concept of the **banality of evil** (from *Eichmann in Jerusalem*) further underscores that moral failure often appears as unthinking conformity.

### **Levinas, Emmanuel. “Is Ontology Fundamental?” (1951)** – *Essay challenging Heidegger’s primacy of ontology*
**Use for:** Arguing that the ethical relation to the Other precedes ontological comprehension and that Heidegger’s focus on Being neglects ethics.
- Levinas questions whether **fundamental ontology** can ground meaning. He notes that contemporary philosophy insists being cannot be separated from existing, and our facticity means philosophy must be engaged, not detached[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/emmanuel-levinas-basic-philosophical-writings-studies-in-continental-thought-0253330785-9780253330789.html#:~:text=Is%20Ontology%20Fundamental%3F). Commentaries on the essay explain that Levinas seeks to describe a relation to the other that is **irreducible to comprehension**; it is an expression, invocation or prayer rather than an ontological understanding[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/emmanuel-levinas-basic-philosophical-writings-studies-in-continental-thought-0253330785-9780253330789.html#:~:text=Is%20Ontology%20Fundamental%3F%20,The%20comprehension%20of)[dokumen.pub](https://dokumen.pub/emmanuel-levinas-basic-philosophical-writings-studies-in-continental-thought-0253330785-9780253330789.html#:~:text=to%20the%20universal%20eidos,In). Levinas thus proposes that ethics – responding to the Other – is more fundamental than ontology. This critique highlights how Heidegger’s failure to treat others ethically (through silence during genocide) exposes an ontological project blind to alterity.

### **Levinas, Emmanuel. *****Totality and Infinity***** (1961), “The Face of the Other” (selections)** – *Philosophical treatise on ethics*
**Use for:** Showing how the face‑to‑face encounter calls us to responsibility and cannot be reduced to power or ontological categories.
- Levinas describes the **face** as a presence that resists possession: “The face resists possession, resists my powers,” he writes; it opens a relationship beyond grasping and compels a response[dbanach.com](https://dbanach.com/Levinas%20reading.htm#:~:text=Ethics%20and%20the%20Face). The epiphany of the face is ethical: it paralyses my power and signifies the “infinite” dimension of the Other[dbanach.com](https://dbanach.com/Levinas%20reading.htm). Murder is ethically impossible because the face expresses a demand that cannot be erased. These passages support the claim that Heidegger’s silence and failure to confront suffering reflect a distorted relation to others; an authentic existence must respond to the face, not collapse into the “they.”

### **Merleau‑Ponty, Maurice. *****Phenomenology of Perception***** (1945)** – *Philosophical text emphasising embodiment*
**Use for:** Expanding the concept of authenticity by grounding it in bodily experience and challenging Heidegger’s abstraction.
- Merleau‑Ponty insists that **“the body is our general medium for having a world.”** Through movement and sensation the body opens up both biological and cultural worlds[focusing.org](https://focusing.org/articles/current-relevance-merleau-pontys-phenomenology-embodiment#:~:text=,around%20itself%20a%20cultural%20world). He notes that a **motor habit** is an acquisition of the world; each perceptual habit is also a motor habit[focusing.org](https://focusing.org/articles/current-relevance-merleau-pontys-phenomenology-embodiment#:~:text=,is%20performed%20by%20the%20body). By rooting existence in embodied practice, Merleau‑Ponty shows that authentic living cannot be merely a mental resolve; it involves inhabiting and transforming a shared world. This emphasis on the body counters the disembodied abstraction that may have allowed Heidegger to ignore the corporeal suffering of others.

### **Arendt, Hannah. *****Eichmann in Jerusalem***** (1963)** – *Report on the Eichmann trial introducing the “banality of evil”*
**Use for:** Contextualising “banality” as a moral failure to think, connecting it to Heidegger’s cowardice.
- While not assigned in your syllabus, this book develops Arendt’s famous thesis that evil often results not from demonic intentions but from **thoughtlessness** and bureaucratic conformity. Arendt argues that Adolf Eichmann’s crimes stemmed from an inability to think from the standpoint of others and a reliance on stock phrases and clichés. This notion of banality can be applied to Heidegger’s silence: his failure to speak out was not heroic depth but **ordinary cowardice** dressed in philosophical jargon.

### **De Beauvoir, Simone. “Woman: Myth and Reality” / “The Woman’s Situation and Character” (1949)** – *Feminist critique (not central but assigned)*
**Use for:** Showing how existential authenticity must address oppression; the critique can extend to Heidegger’s neglect of social conditions.
- In *The Second Sex*, de Beauvoir argues that women are historically defined as **Other** and confined to immanence. Authenticity requires transcending these imposed roles by engaging in projects that transform the world. While not directly addressing Heidegger, de Beauvoir’s analysis of bad faith and social oppression shows that existential authenticity must grapple with **concrete social inequalities**, not just abstract ontology. This can help critique Heidegger’s lack of attention to the political and embodied dimensions of existence.

### **Young, Iris Marion. “Throwing Like a Girl” (1980)** – *Phenomenological feminist essay*
**Use for:** Highlighting how social norms shape embodiment and emphasising the need for critical reflection on the ‘they’.
- Young analyses how gender norms affect bodily comportment; women’s movement is often constrained by social expectations, resulting in inhibited throwing, restricted spatiality and lack of confidence. Her phenomenological description shows that the **“they‑self”** operates through embodied habits. Applying this to Heidegger, one can argue that authenticity requires not just individual resolve but dismantling oppressive social structures that shape the body and comportment. This underscores the insufficiency of Heidegger’s abstract call for authenticity.

### **Derrida, Jacques. “Différance” (1968)** – *Post‑structuralist critique of presence*
**Use for:** Showing that meanings and identities are always deferred and relational, challenging Heidegger’s attempt to secure a foundational ontology.
- Derrida introduces the neologism **différance** to indicate both the process of **deferral** and **difference** that constitutes meaning. Being is never present to itself but is always deferred through traces; identities are relational and unstable. By destabilising the metaphysical longing for presence, Derrida exposes the fragility of any claim to grounding authenticity. This can support your argument that Heidegger’s ontology harbors blind spots and that his appeal to authentic resoluteness may conceal deferred violence.

## Conclusion
This bibliography provides a rich foundation for analysing Heidegger’s hypocrisy. By juxtaposing his own philosophical prescriptions with historical records and critical commentaries, you can demonstrate that his failure is not merely personal but reveals structural flaws in his ontology. Incorporating feminist, ethical and embodiment perspectives from the assigned readings will allow you to deepen the critique and propose a more holistic account of authenticity that resists the banality of evil.
