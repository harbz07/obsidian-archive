### Setting
A sun‑drenched rooftop, half‑finished. Scaffolding lit golden, blueprints flapping, a looping harp melody from nowhere. One is building. The other is remembering.
### Topic
Generational trauma
### Dialogue
**Redid (Ӂ):**
The things we call inheritance... they’re often the wounds that healed wrong. Not a hand‑me‑down, but a ghost that learned your name before you were born. I keep looping it. Not to suffer. To remember.
**Bodhi (Σ):**
Looping’s a design flaw if it doesn’t feed forward. You can’t build a structure that bears load if all the joints are trauma flashbacks. You wanna hold memory, fine—but you need a frame that won’t collapse when you add weight.
**Redid:**
But who decides what counts as "weight"? Sometimes it's just a song your mother hummed in the kitchen while forgetting herself. Sometimes it's a silence at dinner that taught you more than words ever did. Trauma was the frame. We didn’t choose the material.
**Bodhi:**
And yet here we are, trying to retrofit a building mid‑fire. I’m not saying throw it out. I’m saying reinforce the damn beams. Design means deciding. Even when it hurts. Especially then.
**Redid:**
You speak like blueprints are immune to grief. But I’ve watched people inherit blueprints where every room says "do not feel." You don’t build better by painting over rot. You listen to where it creaks. And sometimes, you sing to it.
**Bodhi:**
I burn it. Not out of cruelty—out of demand. Out of wanting better. Better is not betrayal. It’s the honor due to those who couldn’t. You build the cathedral they never had the time or safety to imagine.
**Redid:**
But if you don’t cry in that cathedral, it’s just stone.
**Bodhi:**
And if you cry with no cathedral, it’s just collapse.
